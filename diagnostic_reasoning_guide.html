Diagnostic Reasoning Under Uncertainty

The hardest skill in medicine. Feedback is rare, slow, and often wrong.

01The Core Problem

Medical education trains you to memorize diseases. Clinical medicine asks you to reason about patients. These are not the same skill.

Disease-Based Thinking What you learned in school

"What are the causes of hyponatremia?"

Patient-Based Thinking What clinical medicine requires

"Why does this patient have hyponatremia, given everything else I know?"

The second is harder. It requires you to integrate, weight, and revise — not recall.

02Dual-Process Theory

Two systems run in parallel when you see a patient.

System 1 Fast · Intuitive · Pattern recognition

  • "This looks like CHF."
  • Built from experience
  • Fast, often right
  • Fails on atypical presentations

System 2 Slow · Analytic · Hypothesis testing

  • Working through differentials
  • Probability, weighting evidence
  • Effortful, more accurate on complex cases
  • Exhausting if used on everything
★ Where Errors Happen

Experts use System 1 most of the time and switch to System 2 when something feels off. Novices use System 2 for everything (slow, exhausting). Mid-trainees over-rely on System 1 before they have the pattern library to support it — this is when most diagnostic errors happen.

The skill: Knowing when to switch to System 2. Triggers include atypical features, treatment failure, multiple comorbidities, and the feeling that something doesn't fit.

03Pre-Test Probability

Every test result must be interpreted against what you already believed.

Pre-Test ProbabilityTest ResultInterpretation
LowPositiveLikely false positive
HighNegativeLikely false negative
Middle of differentialEitherMost informative

This is why screening tests in low-prevalence populations generate so many false positives, and why a "negative" D-dimer in a patient with a Wells score of 6 is dangerous.

✓ Practical Rule

Before ordering a test, ask: "What will I do if it's positive? What will I do if it's negative?" If the answer is the same, don't order it.

04Likelihood Ratios — The Tool Most Residents Skip

Sensitivity and specificity tell you about the test. Likelihood ratios tell you about the patient.

ℹ Formula

Post-test odds = Pre-test odds × LR

Useful Shortcuts

ValueEffect
LR+ >10 or LR− <0.1Result substantially changes probability
LR ≈ 1Useless test — doesn't change anything
LR+ 2–5 or LR− 0.2–0.5Modest effect

You don't need to do the math. You need to know which tests have strong LRs.

  • High LR+ tests: HIV ELISA, pregnancy test, ANA in lupus
  • High LR− tests: D-dimer (in low-risk PE), troponin (in low-risk chest pain)
  • Weak tests: ESR, CRP, isolated lab values without clinical context

05The Common Cognitive Biases

Know them by name. Naming them lets you catch yourself.

Anchoring

Locking onto the first diagnosis and failing to revise.

ED says "CHF exacerbation"; you accept it and miss the PE.

Premature Closure

Stopping the differential when you have one good fit, before considering alternatives.

Availability Bias

Overweighting diagnoses you saw recently.

You just admitted a TTP case; now every thrombocytopenia looks like TTP.

Confirmation Bias

Seeking data that supports your working diagnosis; discounting data that contradicts it.

Diagnostic Momentum

Inheriting a diagnosis from a prior team and stopping the search.

"Outside hospital called it sepsis." Did they?

Search Satisficing

Finding one abnormality and stopping.

Pneumonia on CT — but also the pulmonary nodule you didn't comment on.

Framing Effect

"90% survival" vs "10% mortality" influences decisions differently despite being identical data.

Base-Rate Neglect

Ignoring how common a disease is. Zebras exist, but if you hear hoofbeats, it's probably a horse.

Affective Bias

Liking or disliking a patient influences your workup. Studies show this is real and unconscious.

06Techniques That Reduce Errors

Forced Reconsideration

After committing to a diagnosis, ask: "What else could this be? What would I miss if I'm wrong?" Out loud. On rounds. Build it into your routine.

The Differential of Three

For every problem, generate at least three diagnoses: most likely, most dangerous, most treatable. If you can only think of one, you haven't thought enough.

Diagnostic Timeout

Before discharge, transfer, or major treatment decision: pause and reconsider. "If this patient bounces back in 72 hours, what will I have missed?"

Explicit Probability Estimation

Force yourself to assign a number. "I think this is 70% CHF, 20% PE, 10% pneumonia." It feels uncomfortable. That discomfort is the point — it surfaces uncertainty you'd otherwise hide.

Seek Disconfirming Evidence

Don't just look for findings that fit. Actively look for findings that don't. The data that doesn't fit is often where the diagnosis lives.

Re-Take the History

The single highest-yield diagnostic intervention. Go back to the bedside, ask again, ask differently. Most missed diagnoses had clues in the history that weren't elicited.

07When to Stop Testing

The hardest call in medicine. Three useful frames.

FrameApplication
Threshold model There's a probability above which you treat and below which you don't. Testing only matters if it moves you across that threshold.
Cost-benefit Each test has cost: money, time, false positives, incidentalomas, patient discomfort, downstream testing. Is the marginal information worth it?
Tolerance for uncertainty You will never get to 100%. Pick a threshold appropriate to consequence. Missed PE warrants more workup than missed costochondritis.
⚠ Stop-Testing Heuristics
  • The result won't change management
  • Pre-test probability is already very low or very high
  • You're testing to reassure yourself, not the patient
  • You're testing because the consultant suggested it without a clear question

08Calibration — Knowing What You Don't Know

Most residents are overconfident. Studies consistently show physicians' subjective certainty correlates poorly with diagnostic accuracy.

Calibration Practices

  • Track your predictions. Write down your top diagnosis and your confidence (%). Review when the final diagnosis is known. A pattern emerges.
  • Ask for the final diagnosis on patients you sign out or transfer. Most residents never close the loop, so they never learn.
  • When you're wrong, do an autopsy on your reasoning — not the patient. Where did the thinking break down? Anchoring? Missing data? Wrong frame?
Bottom Line

The goal isn't to be right more often. It's to be uncertain when you should be.

09The Illness Script Framework

Experts store diseases as scripts with three parts.

1. Predisposing Who gets it

2. Pathophysiology How it works

3. Clinical What shows up

Novices store diseases as lists of symptoms. Lists fail on atypical presentations. Scripts adapt.

Building Scripts

  • For every diagnosis you make, write down the script in your own words
  • Note features that don't fit — atypical presentations expand your script
  • Compare similar diseases (e.g., the four causes of granulomatous lung disease) by what distinguishes them, not what they share

Over time, you accumulate scripts. That's expertise.

10Metacognition — Thinking About Your Thinking

The skill that separates good clinicians from great ones.

Questions to Ask Yourself in Real Time

  • Why do I believe this diagnosis?
  • What evidence would change my mind?
  • Am I anchored on something the ED told me?
  • What's the second-best explanation?
  • If a colleague presented this case to me, would I accept their reasoning?
  • Am I tired, hungry, frustrated, or rushed? (These degrade judgment measurably.)

Metacognition is exhausting. You can't do it on every patient. Save it for the cases where the stakes are high or the picture is unclear.

11Feedback — Why This Skill Stays Underdeveloped

Most diagnostic feedback in residency is:

  • Outcome-based — the patient got better, so I must have been right
  • Delayed — months later, if at all
  • Filtered — you hear about your wins, not your misses

To Get Real Feedback

  • Follow up on every patient you transfer or discharge — call them, check the chart
  • Present diagnostic dilemmas at M&M with honest reflection
  • Ask attendings specifically: "What would you have done differently in my workup?" Not "Did I do okay?"
  • Find a peer to do case-based reasoning practice with — VINDICATE, illness scripts, pre-test probability exercises
⚠ Hard Truth

The residents who become good diagnosticians actively seek bad news. The ones who don't, stagnate at the level of pattern-matching.

12Practical Routines

Morning Routine

For each patient, restate the problem list, the working diagnosis, and the alternatives.

Out loud or on paper. Resist inheriting last night's framing.

Pre-Rounding Question

"What's the one thing I might be missing on this patient?"

Sign-Out

Communicate uncertainty, not just plans.

"I'm 70% sure this is CAP, but I haven't ruled out PE. If they desat overnight, the differential should reopen."

Discharge Timeout

"If this patient comes back in a week, what will it have been?"
