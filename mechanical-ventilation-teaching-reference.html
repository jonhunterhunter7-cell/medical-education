Part I

Foundations

Build the conceptual base: who needs ventilation and why, the respiratory physiology that ventilator settings interact with, the mechanics of compliance and resistance, and how the lung's pressure-volume curve drives every modern bedside decision.

1Why we ventilate — types of respiratory failure

Mechanical ventilation is, fundamentally, a way to do part of the patient's work of breathing while we treat the underlying problem. Six clinical patterns drive most intubations:

  1. Type I (hypoxemic) failure — arterial partial pressure of O₂ (PaO₂) < 60 mmHg, or peripheral oxygen saturation (SpO₂) < 90% on adequate fraction of inspired O₂ (FiO₂). Mechanism: shunt (acute respiratory distress syndrome [ARDS], pneumonia, edema, atelectasis) or ventilation/perfusion (V/Q) mismatch.
  2. Type II (hypercapnic) failure — arterial partial pressure of CO₂ (PaCO₂) > 45 mmHg with acidosis. Mechanism: failure of the pump (chronic obstructive pulmonary disease [COPD] exacerbation, asthma, neuromuscular weakness, drug overdose, brainstem injury).
  3. Type III (perioperative) — atelectasis-driven hypoxemia in surgical / obese / abdominal patients.
  4. Type IV (shock) — high work of breathing diverts cardiac output from vital organs; intubation off-loads respiratory muscles.
  5. Inability to protect the airway — depressed mental status (Glasgow Coma Scale [GCS] ≤ 8), bulbar dysfunction, ongoing hematemesis.
  6. Anticipated decline — flail chest, escalating sepsis, expanding intracranial process, agitated patient with worsening trajectory.
Physiology noteThe PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio (P/F) is the most-used hypoxemia index because it normalizes for FiO₂. Caveat: P/F is sensitive to PEEP and barometric pressure; the SpO₂/FiO₂ ratio (S/F) is a non-invasive surrogate (S/F ≈ 0.5 × P/F + 100) used in the 2023 Global Definition of ARDS.1
Key takeaways
  • Six clinical patterns drive most intubations: hypoxemic failure, hypercapnic failure, perioperative atelectasis, shock, airway protection, anticipated decline.
  • Most intubations are not for a specific PaO₂/PaCO₂ number — they're for the trajectory and the ability to protect the airway.
  • P/F ratio < 100 = severe ARDS; < 80 = ECMO consideration.
  • "Anticipated decline" is the most under-recognized indication — intubate proactively when the trajectory is bad, not after the patient codes.

2Respiratory physiology essentials

Every ventilator setting acts on physiology. The next 30 pages of West's Respiratory Physiology compressed into a critical-care toolkit — the framework you'll come back to whenever a patient surprises you on the screen.28

2.1 The oxygen cascade — why hypoxemia happens at all

Oxygen falls in pressure at every step from the atmosphere to the mitochondrion. Naming the steps lets you point to where a particular patient's hypoxemia is coming from.

Figure 2.1 — The oxygen cascade. Atmospheric PO₂ (160 mmHg) falls to ~150 once humidified, to ~100 in the alveolus once CO₂ is exchanged, to ~95 arterially (the small A–a gradient is normal V/Q heterogeneity), to ~40 in mixed venous blood, and to a critical mitochondrial PO₂ of 5–20 mmHg. Hypoxemia means a derangement at one of the alveolus → arterial → tissue steps.28

An elevated A–a gradient indicates a problem at the alveolar–capillary level (V/Q mismatch, shunt, diffusion impairment). A normal A–a gradient with hypoxemia means hypoventilation or low FiO₂ — the alveolus is fine, it just isn't being filled with O₂.

Each term, broken out: FiO₂ is the inspired oxygen fraction (0.21 room air → 1.0 pure O₂); Patm is atmospheric pressure (760 mmHg at sea level, less at altitude); PH₂O is water-vapor pressure at body temperature (47 mmHg, fully saturated airway gas); PaCO₂ is the patient's measured arterial CO₂; R is the respiratory exchange ratio, ~ 0.8 in steady state. The formula computes the PO₂ that should be in the alveolus; subtracting measured PaO₂ gives the A–a gradient — the amount of oxygen "lost" between alveolus and artery, which scales the diagnosis of hypoxemia.

2.4 V/Q matching and West's three-zone model

West's classic insight (1962): in the upright lung, alveolar pressure (PA), arterial pressure (Pa), and venous pressure (Pv) interact differently at different heights, producing three zones of perfusion.28 Mechanical ventilation — particularly with PEEP — can convert physiologic Zone 2 into pathologic Zone 1 (dead space).

Figure 2.2 — West's three-zone model. Apical alveoli see alveolar pressure greater than capillary pressure (Zone 1, dead space — none in healthy upright lungs but created by hypovolemia or excess PEEP). Mid-lung Zone 2 has waterfall behavior. Basal Zone 3 has continuous flow. Adding PEEP shifts more lung into Zone 1 — the price of recruitment is dead space and right-heart afterload.28

The vertical V/Q ratio in a healthy upright lung is ~3.3 at the apex (high V/Q — wasted ventilation) to ~0.6 at the base (low V/Q — preferentially perfused). Mechanical ventilation, supine positioning, and PEEP all redistribute this gradient.

2.4a Hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction (HPV) — the body's V/Q regulator

When alveolar PO₂ drops in a region of lung, the local pulmonary arterioles constrict, diverting blood away from the poorly-ventilated unit toward better-ventilated lung. This hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction (HPV) is a uniquely pulmonary reflex (systemic arteries dilate to hypoxia; pulmonary arteries constrict). It is the body's automatic V/Q matching system — and it explains a striking number of clinical observations:

  • Why proning works (§19.5, §32): dorsal lung in supine ARDS is atelectatic and locally hypoxic; HPV diverts blood away to ventral lung. Once prone, the dorsal lung re-aerates → HPV releases → blood returns to the now-better-ventilated dependent regions. V/Q matching improves regardless of any change in vent settings.
  • Why iNO works (§19.7): inhaled nitric oxide reaches only the alveoli that are ventilated, where it diffuses into adjacent capillaries and selectively dilates them — overcoming HPV in good units, redistributing flow toward them, and dropping PVR. NO inhaled into a shunt unit goes nowhere because there's no gas exchange there.
  • Why hyperoxia raises PaCO₂ in COPD (§20.6): high FiO₂ raises alveolar PO₂ in poorly-ventilated regions, releasing HPV → blood flows there → those regions become high-V/Q dead space → effective alveolar ventilation drops → PaCO₂ rises. (The Haldane effect is the second mechanism.)
  • Why severe lung disease causes pulmonary hypertension and cor pulmonale: chronic widespread alveolar hypoxia → sustained HPV → vascular remodeling → fixed pulm HTN → RV failure (§24).
  • Why high-altitude pulmonary edema happens: hypoxia-induced HPV is heterogeneous; over-perfused non-constricted regions overpressurize and leak.
The unifying lessonHPV is the single physiologic concept that ties together V/Q matching → proning → iNO → COPD hyperoxia → cor pulmonale → high-altitude edema. When you find yourself thinking about any of these, you're really thinking about HPV.

  • ARDS phenotypes (§19.1) — focal vs diffuse maps onto regional HPV

When PaCO₂ rises after a PEEP increase, you've crossed into over-distension. The ETCO₂ trace is your real-time monitor.

2.6 Shunt vs V/Q mismatch — the hypoxemia dichotomy

True (anatomic) shunt — perfusion of alveoli with V/Q = 0. The blood traversing them sees no O₂ at any FiO₂. Hypoxemia resists supplemental O₂. Causes: ARDS, lobar pneumonia, atelectasis, AVM, intracardiac right-to-left shunt.

V/Q mismatch (low-V/Q units) — perfusion of poorly-ventilated alveoli (V/Q < 1, but ≠ 0). Hypoxemia responds well to supplemental O₂ because raising inspired PO₂ overcomes the low ventilation. Causes: COPD, asthma, PE (compensatory), bronchospasm.

The shunt equation (Berggren) quantifies the shunt fraction:

$$\frac{\dot{Q}_s}{\dot{Q}_t} \;=\; \frac{C_cO_2 - C_aO_2}{C_cO_2 - C_vO_2}$$

where Cc, Ca, Cv = end-capillary, arterial, mixed-venous O₂ content. Bedside estimate: PaO₂/FiO₂ < 200 implies shunt > ~20%; < 100 implies shunt > ~30%.

Figure 2.4 — The Berggren shunt equation, visualized. Mixed venous blood (CvO₂, left) enters the pulmonary circulation; the fraction Qs bypasses gas exchange entirely (lower path) while the rest passes through ventilated alveoli, equilibrating with alveolar gas (ctO₂(A) ≈ CcO₂, end-pulmonary-capillary content). The two streams mix to produce arterial blood (CaO₂, right). The numerator (CcO₂ − CaO₂) is the oxygen the shunt fraction should have picked up but did not; the denominator (CcO₂ − CvO₂) is the oxygen each unit of fully-oxygenated blood actually picks up. Their ratio is the shunt fraction Qs/Qt.

2.7 The oxygen content equation — saturation matters more than tension

$$C_aO_2 \;=\; (1.34 \times \text{Hb} \times S_aO_2) \;+\; (0.003 \times P_aO_2)$$

The dissolved-O₂ term (0.003 × PaO₂) is trivial at normal hemoglobin. Saturation × hemoglobin carries 98% of arterial O₂. Implications:

  • Once SpO₂ > 90%, further PaO₂ rises do almost nothing for tissue delivery — but they accumulate oxygen toxicity.
  • A patient with Hb 7 has half the O₂ content of a patient with Hb 14 at the same SpO₂. For tissue O₂ delivery, transfuse before maximizing FiO₂.
Figure 2.5 — The oxygen delivery (DO₂) equation, annotated. DO₂ = CO × ((1.39 × [Hb] × SaO₂) + (PaO₂ × 0.03)). Cardiac output (L/min) multiplies the total arterial oxygen content. Within the content term, the first product (Hb × SaO₂ × 1.39) is the bound-to-hemoglobin contribution — dominant at any normal hemoglobin level — and the second product (PaO₂ × 0.03) is the dissolved oxygen, trivial at normal Hb. The constant 1.39 is Hüfner's number, the maximal oxygen-carrying capacity of fully saturated hemoglobin in mL O₂ per gram Hb. The three independent levers are CO, Hb, and SaO₂; the ventilator moves only SaO₂.

2.8 The oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve — Bohr effect

Figure 2.3 — The oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve. The "edge of the cliff" sits at PaO₂ 60 / SaO₂ 90 — small drops below this cause big saturation drops. Why the curve is sigmoidHemoglobin's four binding sites cooperate (positive cooperativity): the first O₂ molecule changes conformation to make subsequent binding easier. The result is a curve that's flat at the top (so PaO₂ can fall from 100 → 70 with little SaO₂ change) and steep in the middle (so once SaO₂ drops below 90%, every further mmHg matters).

2.9 CO₂ transport and the Haldane effect

CO₂ travels in three forms: dissolved (~10%), as bicarbonate (~70%), and bound to hemoglobin as carbamino compounds (~20%). The Haldane effect is the mirror image of the Bohr effect: deoxygenated hemoglobin binds more CO₂. So in tissues, hemoglobin gives up O₂ and picks up more CO₂; in the lung, oxygenation displaces CO₂ off hemoglobin to be exhaled.

Clinical consequence: a patient with anemia (reduced Hb buffering) tolerates respiratory acidosis less well; massive transfusion can transiently worsen it via citrate-induced metabolic alkalosis but improve CO₂ buffering.

Figure 2.6 — Bohr and Haldane effects, side by side. Left (Bohr, peripheral tissues): high CO₂, H⁺, and temperature shift the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve right — H⁺ binds histidine residues, stabilizes the deoxy (T) state, lowers O₂ affinity, and unloads O₂ to metabolically active tissues. Right (Haldane, pulmonary capillary): high PO₂ shifts the CO₂ dissociation curve left — O₂ binds heme iron, stabilizes the oxy (R) state, lowers CO₂ and H⁺ binding, and unloads CO₂ for exhalation. The two effects are mirror images linked by hemoglobin's allosteric switching between T and R conformations.

2.10 Diffusion vs perfusion limitation

Most gases (O₂ in health, N₂O, CO₂) are perfusion-limited — equilibration between alveolar gas and pulmonary capillary blood happens within the first third of capillary transit. Increasing blood flow raises uptake.

In disease, O₂ becomes diffusion-limited: when the alveolar–capillary membrane is thickened (fibrosis, ARDS) or transit time shortened (exercise, hyperdynamic state), capillary PO₂ never equilibrates with alveolar PO₂. The result: exertional hypoxemia in fibrosis, hypoxemia worsened by sepsis-induced high cardiac output in ARDS.28

2.11 Control of breathing

Three sensors and one central pattern generator drive ventilation:

  • Central chemoreceptors (medullary surface) sense CSF pH, which tracks PaCO₂ via free diffusion of CO₂ across the blood-brain barrier. This is the dominant minute-to-minute drive in health.
  • Peripheral chemoreceptors (carotid + aortic bodies) sense PaO₂, PaCO₂, and pH. They become the dominant drive when PaO₂ < 60 mmHg, in chronic CO₂ retention, and when central drive is depressed (sedation, hypothyroidism).
  • Stretch receptors (Hering-Breuer reflex) terminate inspiration at large lung volumes — the Cushing-like reflex behind some asynchrony.

In the ICU, the implications are practical: over-sedation suppresses central drive (RR drops, PaCO₂ rises, dyssynchrony with the vent appears as missed triggers); metabolic acidosis drives a tachypneic respiratory compensation that the vent must accommodate (high-rate alarm misuse causes auto-PEEP); chronic CO₂ retention resets central thresholds — overzealous correction back to normal PaCO₂ causes alkalosis-induced hypoventilation when sedated.

The chronic CO₂ retainer's ventFor a COPD patient with baseline PaCO₂ 60, target PaCO₂ at their baseline, not 40. Acute correction to 40 produces metabolic alkalosis, hypokalemia, weakness, and seizure risk (Owens, Hess).30
Key takeaways
  • The five causes of hypoxemia: hypoventilation, low FiO₂, V/Q mismatch, shunt, diffusion impairment. Only shunt fails to correct on 100% O₂.
  • A–a gradient elevated → alveolar-capillary problem (V/Q, shunt, diffusion). A–a normal with hypoxemia → hypoventilation or low FiO₂.
  • Saturation × hemoglobin carries 98% of arterial O₂ content. Dissolved O₂ (0.003 × PaO₂) is trivial — don't chase PaO₂ > 80 with high FiO₂.
  • "Edge of the cliff": SpO₂ 90% ≈ PaO₂ 60. Below this, small drops in PaO₂ cause big drops in saturation.
  • Bohr: high CO₂/H⁺/T → right-shifts oxy-Hb curve → unloads O₂ to tissues. Haldane: high O₂ → loads CO₂ off Hb in lungs. They are mirror effects of allosteric switching.
  • Three drives: central chemoreceptors (PaCO₂ — dominant), peripheral chemoreceptors (PaO₂ < 60, chronic retention), stretch receptors. Don't normalize PaCO₂ to 40 in a chronic retainer.

FRC is the most clinically important capacity for ventilator decisions — atelectasis develops below FRC, dynamic hyperinflation accumulates above it.

3.1a Closing capacity vs FRC — why some patients desaturate fast

Closing capacity (CC) is the lung volume below which small airways start to collapse on exhalation. FRC is the normal end-expiratory volume. The relationship between these two — not the absolute number of either — predicts who develops atelectasis and shunt.

  • Healthy young upright adult: FRC > CC by ~ 1 L. Tidal breathing happens entirely above closing volume; no atelectasis develops.
  • Aging: CC rises by ~ 50 mL/year of life. By age 65 supine, CC and FRC roughly equal. Tidal breaths now drop below closing volume → small-airway collapse → V/Q low units → relative hypoxemia even in healthy elderly.
  • Obesity: FRC drops dramatically (up to 50%) due to abdominal-wall compliance and supine diaphragm displacement. CC unchanged → CC > FRC → tidal breaths happen partly inside collapsed airway zone → shunt at baseline.
  • Supine position: FRC drops ~ 20% supine vs upright. Combined with anesthesia and induction agents (which further reduce FRC by ~ 20%), the supine obese anesthetized patient has FRC well below CC within seconds.
  • Acute lung injury: edema and surfactant loss raise CC and lower FRC simultaneously — atelectasis everywhere.
Why the obese patient desaturates in 60 secondsFRC has dropped to ~ 30% of predicted; closing capacity is unchanged or higher; almost the entire tidal range is below closing volume. There's almost no oxygen reservoir to draw from during apnea. This is why pre-oxygenation, ramping, and apneic oxygenation are mandatory — and why even brief desaturation occurs minutes after induction in non-obese patients but seconds after in obese patients.
The clinical lever: PEEP raises FRC above CCThat's the single most important physiologic effect of PEEP — not just "open the alveolus," but "lift the operating volume back above closing capacity." This is why PEEP 8–12 helps the obese anesthetized patient even without ARDS, why post-operative atelectasis is treated with CPAP, and why supine position alone causes shunt that PEEP can fix.

3.2 Compliance

Compliance (C) = ΔV/ΔP. Two flavors:

  • Static compliance (Cstat) = VT / (Pplat − PEEP), where Pplat = plateau pressure (the elastic-recoil pressure with no flow) — pure elastic lung+chest-wall; measured during a 0.5-s end-inspiratory hold with no flow. Normal ≈ 60–100 mL/cmH₂O; ARDS often 20–40.
  • Dynamic compliance = VT / (PIP − PEEP), where PIP = peak inspiratory pressure — includes resistance; used for trending only.

3.3 Resistance

$$R_{aw} \;=\; \frac{P_{IP} - P_{plat}}{\dot{V}} \quad \text{(constant-flow VC; }\dot{V}\text{ in L/sec)}$$

Normal airway resistance (Raw) < 5 cmH₂O/L/sec on a vented patient. Elevated Raw points to an endotracheal tube (ETT) issue (kink, secretions, biting), bronchospasm, or upper-airway obstruction. Note the trick: if PIP is high but Pplat is normal, the problem is resistance, not the lung parenchyma.

3.4 Time constants

$$\tau \;=\; R \times C \quad \text{(time constant, in seconds)}$$

The time constant is the time required for an exponentially-decaying lung unit to empty (or fill) by ~63%. After 1τ the unit has emptied 63%; after 2τ, 86%; after 3τ, 95%; after 5τ, ≥ 99%. The same math governs filling on inspiration. The clinical rule of thumb: full equilibration requires 3–5 τ.

3.4.1 Why time constants matter clinically
  • Auto-PEEP / dynamic hyperinflation — in severe COPD/asthma, R is markedly elevated → τ > 1.5–2 s. If the ventilator's expiratory time (Te) is shorter than 3τ, the lung cannot empty before the next breath; gas trapping and intrinsic PEEP develop. This is the physiologic justification for low respiratory rate (8–10) and prolonged I:E (1:4–1:5) in obstructed patients (Ch 20).
  • Recruitment heterogeneity in ARDS — ARDS lungs have regionally different time constants. Healthy alveoli have short τ (low R, normal C). Atelectatic regions have long τ (need higher pressure for longer to recruit). Overdistended regions have short τ but already at the top of the compliance curve. Single-PEEP solutions are always compromises; this is why personalized titration (best-compliance, esophageal-guided) outperforms one-size-fits-all approaches.
  • Cycling-threshold tuning in PSV — the expiratory cycle threshold (default 25% of peak inspiratory flow) effectively defines when the ventilator decides inspiration "should" be over. In long-τ patients (ARDS), inspiration may need a higher cycle threshold (35–40%) to terminate appropriately; in short-τ patients (COPD), a lower threshold (10–15%).
  • Pressure-control inspiratory time — in PC mode, set Ti ≈ 3–5 τ for full equilibration of pressure into the alveolus. Shorter Ti = lower delivered VT than expected; longer Ti wastes time without adding volume.
3.4.2 Measuring τ at the bedside

Modern ventilators do not display τ directly, but you can compute or visualize it from waveforms and bedside maneuvers:

  1. From the equation — τ (sec) = Raw (cmH₂O/L/sec) × Cstat (L/cmH₂O). With Raw from the PIP–Pplat gradient (resistive pressure / inspiratory flow) and Cstat = VT/(Pplat − PEEP). Example: VT 0.5 L, Pplat 22, PEEP 8, PIP 32, flow 1 L/sec → Raw = 10/1 = 10; Cstat = 0.5/14 ≈ 0.036 L/cmH₂O; τ = 10 × 0.036 = 0.36 sec. Te should be ≥ 1 sec to ensure complete emptying.
  2. From the expiratory flow trace — the time from peak expiratory flow to the point where flow has decayed to 37% of peak (i.e., 1 − 1/e) equals the expiratory time constant. Visually: if expiratory flow is still > 0 at the next breath, τ > available Te — auto-PEEP is occurring (Ch 16).
  3. From an end-expiratory pause maneuver — measures total PEEP. Total PEEP − set PEEP = auto-PEEP. Auto-PEEP > 0 means τ exceeded Te.
  4. From volume re-expansion on disconnect — disconnect the patient from the vent for 5–10 seconds. The amount of additional gas that flows out is the trapped volume. (Useful in catastrophic auto-PEEP / hemodynamic collapse — Ch 20.4.)
  5. Stress-index method — under constant-flow VC, the shape of the pressure-time curve reflects whether tidal breathing is occurring on the linear portion of the compliance curve (stress index ≈ 1) or above it (stress index > 1, overdistension) or below it (stress index < 1, recruitment ongoing). Indirectly informs τ regional heterogeneity.
The bedside τ checkIf you suspect auto-PEEP: pause the ventilator's flow trace and look at exhalation. Flow should reach zero before the next inspiration (≥ 3τ exhalation time). If it doesn't, you're hyperinflating — lower the rate or VT, lengthen Te, or both. This single visual check substitutes for the math in > 90% of bedside cases.
Key takeaways
  • The equation of motion (P_aw = V/C + V̇×R + PEEP) explains every bedside pressure: elastic + resistive + baseline.
  • Compliance (Cstat) and resistance (Raw) decompose PIP into lung and airway problems — high PIP with normal Pplat = resistance; both high = stiff lung.
  • Time constants (τ = R × C) dictate whether the lung empties in time; auto-PEEP occurs when expiratory time < 3τ.
  • Closing capacity vs FRC: when FRC drops below CC, tidal breathing happens inside collapsed airways — PEEP's job is to raise FRC above CC.

4The lung's pressure–volume curve

Figure 1. Anatomy of the dynamic pressure-volume loop. The shaded inflation curve has two key inflection points: the lower inflection point (LIP) — the minimum pressure required for alveolar recruitment — and the upper inflection point (UIP) — the pressure at which regional overdistension begins. The flattening above the UIP is called "beaking." The pressure axis can be divided into four operational zones: PEEP (baseline); alveolar recruitment + airway resistance; the optimal-compliance segment between LIP and UIP (where tidal breaths should sit); and overdistension. Tidal volume is the vertical excursion across this loop.

Clinical corollaries:

  • Set PEEP so that tidal breaths sit on the linear-compliance portion of the curve (above LIP, below UIP).
  • Hysteresis is why a brief recruitment maneuver, followed by a decremental PEEP titration, keeps lung open at lower PEEP than was required to open it.
  • The total respiratory-system P-V curve includes the chest wall — in obesity, ascites, ARDS with abdominal hypertension, the chest wall component dominates and PEEP requirements rise.

4.2 The Campbell diagram — separating the lung from the chest wall

The single static P-V curve above shows the respiratory system as a whole. To think clearly about heart-lung interaction, it helps to break that curve into its two components — the lung and the chest wall — and superimpose them. This is the Campbell diagram (E.J.M. Campbell, 1958), the most under-used teaching tool in mechanical ventilation.25

Figure 1b. The Campbell diagram with work of breathing decomposed. The elastic recoil curve of the lung (blue, declining left) and that of the chest wall (red, sigmoid) intersect at functional residual capacity (FRC). Tidal breathing displaces the lung above FRC by the tidal volume shown on the right. The shaded loop in the lower-left depicts the resistive work the inspiratory muscles must perform on each breath — divided into work against airway resistance (e.g., bronchospasm) and work against apparatus resistance (e.g., the endotracheal tube). Figure 1c. The Campbell diagram applied to dynamic hyperinflation (the auto-PEEP physiology of severe COPD or asthma). The patient's operating tidal range is shifted up the curves to a higher lung volume — a "dynamic FRC" sits above the relaxed FRC because expiration is incomplete. Two consequences are visible: (1) elastic work increases because the lung is operating on the steeper, less-compliant portion of the lung-recoil curve; and (2) resistive work increases on both inspiration and expiration because of the added airway obstruction. The patient pays an enormous work-of-breathing penalty just to maintain ventilation at this higher operating volume — the physiologic basis for the inspiratory threshold load that drives ineffective triggering (§17.3.1).
Why this matters at the bedside
  • Pleural pressure (P_pl) is the bridge to the heart. What the lung "sees" mechanically and what the heart "sees" hemodynamically share the same pressure space. The Campbell diagram makes this explicit: every breath shifts P_pl, and that shift loads or unloads each cardiac chamber (see §29.4).
  • An isolated drop in chest-wall compliance (e.g., obesity, ascites, abdominal hypertension) shifts the chest-wall curve to the right and increases P_mus markedly, but barely changes alveolar pressure. The patient feels heavier-to-breathe but their lung is fine — important when interpreting Pplat in obese ARDS (use esophageal manometry; see §29).
  • An isolated drop in lung compliance (e.g., ARDS, edema, fibrosis) shifts the lung curve right; FRC falls; P_mus rises rapidly with volume; the patient compensates with rapid-shallow breathing.
  • Both curves shift right (the typical ICU patient — ARDS plus edema plus ascites) — FRC drops, P_mus rises sharply, work of breathing dominates, and rapid-shallow breathing predicts weaning failure (high RSBI).
  • Passive ventilation moves the patient along the chest-wall curve while the ventilator does the elastic work; the relevant pressure is now airway pressure minus P_pl = transpulmonary pressure (§29).
Key takeaways
  • The P-V curve has a lower inflection point (LIP) where recruitment begins and an upper inflection point (UIP) where overdistension starts — tidal breaths belong between them.
  • The Campbell diagram separates lung from chest wall compliance; high Pplat from chest-wall stiffness (obesity, ascites) doesn't mean lung is injured.
  • Hysteresis is why a recruitment maneuver + decremental PEEP keeps lung open at lower PEEP than was needed to open it.
  • PEEP is only one part of the system; the elastic recoil of both lung and chest wall determines the operating point.

6.3 The difficult airway — predict, plan, fail safely

Predictors (LEMON):

  • Look externally — facial trauma, beard, large tongue, short neck, dental disease
  • Evaluate 3-3-2 — 3 fingerbreadths mouth opening; 3 fingerbreadths chin to hyoid; 2 fingerbreadths hyoid to thyroid notch
  • Mallampati class III–IV (only soft palate / hard palate visible)
  • Obstruction / obesity — angioedema, mass, abscess, BMI > 35
  • Neck mobility limited — c-collar, ankylosing spondylitis, RA

Difficult-airway plan (have all three ready before any anticipated difficult intubation):

  1. Plan A: Direct or video laryngoscopy with optimal positioning + bougie
  2. Plan B: Supraglottic device (LMA, i-gel) — temporary oxygenation/ventilation while regrouping
  3. Plan C: Awake fiberoptic intubation if predictable in advance
  4. Plan D ("can't intubate, can't oxygenate" — CICO): Surgical cricothyrotomy. Don't delay. If supraglottic device fails to oxygenate and patient is desaturating < 80% on max efforts, cut.
The "just one more attempt" trapEach failed laryngoscopy attempt causes airway trauma, edema, bleeding, and desaturation — making the next attempt harder. Three attempts maximum by the same operator before escalating (different operator, different blade, video, supraglottic, surgical). Multiple-attempt intubation is independently associated with worse outcomes.

6.4 Post-intubation hypotension — anticipate, don't react

Hypotension within 10 minutes of intubation occurs in ~30% of ED/ICU intubations and is associated with mortality. Three mechanisms — usually concurrent:

  1. Loss of sympathetic tone from induction agents (especially propofol) — vasoplegia, ↓ preload
  2. Loss of negative-pressure inspiration — switching from spontaneous (which augments venous return) to positive-pressure ventilation drops preload
  3. Underlying disease — sepsis, hypovolemia, RV failure (especially massive PE — see §24.4)

Pre-intubation: shock index (HR/SBP) > 0.8 predicts post-intubation hypotension. Pre-treat with crystalloid bolus + push-dose pressor.

Sedation choice matters — switch from propofol to a less-vasoplegic regimen (fentanyl + dexmedetomidine, or low-dose ketamine drip in shock).

The ventilator

Equipment fluency: how a breath is constructed by the machine, what each control does, the conventional and modern modes, and a defensible day-1 setup that keeps you out of trouble.

7Anatomy of a ventilator breath

Every breath has three phases the operator can think about:

  1. Trigger — how the breath starts. Time-triggered (mandatory rate) or patient-triggered (pressure or flow drop in the circuit, with sensitivity set 1–3 cmH₂O or 1–3 L/min).
  2. Target / limit — what the inspiration shoots for. In volume-targeted breaths, flow and volume are set, pressure is the dependent variable. In pressure-targeted breaths, the inspiratory pressure is fixed and volume is the dependent variable.
  3. Cycle — what ends inspiration. Volume cycle (delivered tidal volume VT reached), time cycle (set inspiratory time Ti elapses; the inspiratory:expiratory time ratio is written I:E), or flow cycle (typically 25% of peak inspiratory flow in pressure support ventilation [PSV]).

Layered on top: the baseline (PEEP) and the FiO₂.

The "break point" at the start marks the abrupt jump from PEEP through the airway-resistance pressure (flow × R) before linear elastic loading begins. At end-inspiration, the brief "plateau decay" from peak to plateau is the resistive component (the lung re-equilibrates once flow stops). The total pressure decomposes vertically into three stacked components: airway-resistance pressure (PIP − Pplat) on top, alveolar-compliance pressure (Pplat − PEEP, the elastic / driving pressure) in the middle, and PEEP at the bottom. Reading these components diagnoses 90% of the "high pressure alarm" cases at the bedside.
Key takeaways
  • Every breath has three phases: trigger (time or pressure drop), target (volume vs pressure), cycle (volume reached, time elapsed, or flow decay).
  • The resistive break-point on the pressure curve marks where flow overcomes airway resistance; the plateau shows elastic recoil alone (flow = 0).
  • Decomposing PIP vertically: airway-resistance pressure (PIP − Pplat) on top, alveolar-compliance pressure (Pplat − PEEP) in middle, PEEP at bottom.
  • Reading these three components diagnoses 90% of high-pressure alarms without further testing.

8Volume control (VC, assist-control VC [A/C-VC])

You set VT, RR, peak flow, flow pattern (square or decelerating), Ti or I:E, PEEP, FiO₂, sensitivity. The machine guarantees the volume; airway pressure becomes the patient. Assist-control means every patient effort triggers a full machine breath.

Pros
  • Guaranteed minute ventilation — useful when CO₂ control matters (ARDS with permissive hypercapnia targets, status asthmaticus, neurological injury).
  • PIP and Pplat reflect the lung's mechanics — easy to read, easy to track.
Cons
  • If lung mechanics worsen suddenly (mucus plug, pneumothorax), pressures spike — risk of barotrauma if alarms aren't set.
  • A square flow pattern can be uncomfortable in the awake patient and a common cause of "flow starvation" asynchrony — switch to decelerating flow if seen.
When to reach for VCARDS (Pplat-driven targets), severe asthma/COPD (control RR and Ti precisely), elevated intracranial pressure (ICP), post-cardiac-arrest with strict PaCO₂ goals.

  • Constant square-flow VC can cause discomfort and trigger asynchrony (flow starvation) in awake patients; decelerating flow is gentler.
  • PIP and Pplat directly read the lung's mechanics and track injury; every pressure spike is visible.
  • Risk: PIP climbs suddenly (PTX, mucus plug, barotrauma) unless alarms are set appropriately.

9Pressure control (PC, assist-control PC [A/C-PC])

You set inspiratory pressure (above PEEP), Ti, RR, PEEP, FiO₂, sensitivity. The machine guarantees the airway pressure; tidal volume becomes the patient.

Pros
  • Decelerating flow pattern is intrinsic — better gas distribution, often more comfortable.
  • Pressure ceiling protects against barotrauma.
Cons
  • Tidal volume varies breath-to-breath with mechanics and patient effort — vigilance for hypoventilation if compliance worsens.
  • If the patient pulls hard, delivered VT can swing widely (patient self-inflicted lung injury [P-SILI] risk in severe ARDS).

For most adult intensive care unit (ICU) patients there is no mortality difference between VC and PC if targets (Pplat, VT) are met.2 Choice is institutional preference and operator comfort.

Figure 7 — Pressure control breath, isolated. A single PC breath. Pressure is the set variable — a square plateau held for the inspiratory time Ti. Key takeaways
  • Pressure control guarantees airway pressure (ceiling protects against barotrauma), but VT varies breath-to-breath with mechanics and patient effort.
  • Decelerating flow pattern is intrinsic to PC, improving gas distribution and comfort vs square VC flow.
  • Disadvantage: vigilance required for hypoventilation if compliance worsens; large patient efforts can cause P-SILI in severe ARDS.
  • No mortality difference between VC and PC if Pplat and VT targets are met — choose based on institutional preference and comfort.

10Pressure support (PSV) and SIMV

10.1 Pressure support

A spontaneous mode: every breath is patient-triggered, pressure-targeted, flow-cycled (default 25% of peak inspiratory flow). The clinician sets pressure-support (PS) level (e.g., 5–15 cmH₂O), PEEP, FiO₂, sensitivity, and the cycle threshold.

PSV is the workhorse for spontaneous breathing trials (SBTs), day-2+ ventilation in cooperative patients, and as a "rest" mode after a tired SBT. Tidal volume should remain < 8 mL/kg of predicted body weight (PBW); if VT explodes, the patient is too well-supported and may be at risk for atelectrauma upon abrupt drop, or P-SILI from large efforts.

Figure 8a — Pressure support breath, isolated. Each PSV breath is patient-triggered (the small pressure dip at start), pressure-targeted (rises to the set PS level above PEEP and holds), and flow-cycled (terminates when inspiratory flow decays to a set percentage of peak — typically 25%, adjustable 5–70%). The patient controls breath timing AND duration; the ventilator just amplifies their effort. Tidal volume is fully dependent — varies with patient effort, lung compliance, and PS level. Cycle threshold tuning is the highest-yield knob: too low (e.g., 10% in ARDS) → late cycling and patient effort to exhale; too high (e.g., 40% in COPD) → early cycling and double-triggering.

10.2 SIMV (synchronized intermittent mandatory ventilation)

A hybrid: a set rate of mandatory (volume- or pressure-targeted) breaths is delivered, synchronized to the patient's effort. Spontaneous breaths in between are pressure-supported.

Modern pulmonary & critical-care medicine (PCCM) practice has largely abandoned SIMV for routine use — multiple randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and meta-analyses showed prolonged liberation compared with PSV-based weaning.3 SIMV remains useful in select pediatric and post-op contexts.

As the time-cycled breath interval ends, the ventilator opens a brief SIM trigger window — if the patient triggers within it, that effort is amplified into a mandatory volume-targeted breath; if not, the ventilator delivers it on its own. SIMV is intuitive on a waveform but, as text notes, has been outperformed by PSV-based weaning in adult RCTs.3

Key takeaways
  • PSV is patient-triggered, pressure-targeted, and flow-cycled (default 25% peak flow) — the workhorse for SBTs and day-2+ weaning.
  • Cycle-threshold tuning is the highest-yield knob: too low in ARDS causes late cycling and dyssynchrony; too high in COPD causes premature cycling.
  • SIMV has been outperformed by PSV-based weaning in adult RCTs (prolonged liberation); largely abandoned for routine use.
  • VT should remain < 8 mL/kg PBW on PSV; exploding VT means over-support and risk of atelectrauma upon sudden drop.

11Adaptive modes & APRV

11.1 Pressure-regulated volume control (PRVC) / VC+ / Autoflow

Closed-loop hybrids: pressure-controlled breaths whose inspiratory pressure is adjusted breath-to-breath to deliver a set target tidal volume. Combines the comfort of PC with a VT guarantee. Caveat: in a strongly breathing patient, the algorithm can drop the support inappropriately, prolonging weaning.

11.2 Airway pressure release ventilation (APRV)

A high continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) at Phigh, interrupted by brief, periodic releases to Plow. Spontaneous breathing is encouraged throughout. Settings:

  • Phigh: 25–30 cmH₂O (titrate to Pplat < 30 if measured during a hold).
  • Plow: 0 cmH₂O (avoids exhaling against pressure but de-recruitment is prevented by the short Tlow).
  • Thigh: 4–6 s (time at Phigh).
  • Tlow: 0.4–0.8 s (release time); titrate so expiratory flow terminates at ~75% of peak.

Used as a rescue or alternative in moderate-severe ARDS at experienced centers; randomized data are mixed, with some signal for improved oxygenation and shorter ventilator days.4

11.3 Neurally adjusted ventilatory assist (NAVA) and proportional assist ventilation (PAV+)

Proportional modes that scale support to either the diaphragm electrical signal (NAVA) or the patient's instantaneous effort (PAV+).

  • NAVA: a specialized nasogastric catheter detects diaphragm electromyographic signal (Edi). The ventilator delivers pressure proportional to Edi — the patient controls timing AND magnitude. Asynchrony rates drop dramatically; trigger latency near-zero (no pneumatic delay). Best for difficult-to-wean patients with significant asynchrony.
  • PAV+: continuously measures patient compliance and resistance and provides a percentage of the work the patient is "asking for." Like NAVA, asynchrony improves; unlike NAVA, no special catheter required.
  • Limitations: requires cooperative patient; not for deeply sedated/paralyzed; learning curve for staff; institution-dependent availability; outcomes data limited.

Reading the ventilator

The information density at the bedside is on the screen, not in the chart. Six waveforms and three numbers will tell you almost everything you need.

13Waveform scalars — the three traces

Modern vents display, top-to-bottom, pressure-time, flow-time, and volume-time. Train your eye to scan in this order:

  1. Pressure: PIP, Pplat (visible if a hold is set), PEEP, shape during inspiration.
  2. Flow: peak inspiratory flow, decel pattern, return to zero before the next breath (no return = auto-PEEP).
  3. Volume: VT delivered, return to baseline (loss of expired volume = leak).

Each shape carries diagnosis. A flow-time curve with a "scooped" inspiratory limb suggests flow starvation (patient pulling more than the vent gives). A pressure-time scalar with a notch or dip mid-inspiration suggests an effort that the vent is undershooting. We'll work through these in §17.

16Auto-PEEP — detection and management

Auto-PEEP (intrinsic PEEP, PEEPi) is alveolar pressure at end-expiration that exceeds the set PEEP, due to incomplete emptying. It causes (a) increased work of triggering for the patient, (b) hemodynamic compromise from raised intrathoracic pressure, and (c) underestimation of mechanics.

How to detect
  • Look at the flow-time trace: expiratory flow does not return to zero before the next breath.
  • Perform an end-expiratory hold: close the expiratory valve for 0.5–1 s; airway pressure equilibrates with alveolar pressure. Total PEEP − set PEEP = auto-PEEP.
Figure 3. Auto-PEEP and dynamic hyperinflation across three simultaneous traces. Top (pressure): end-expiratory alveolar pressure rises progressively breath-over-breath — this is intrinsic PEEP. Middle (flow): expiratory flow does not return to zero before the next inspiration — gas trapping. Bottom (volume): end-expiratory lung volume escalates with each breath — dynamic hyperinflation. The vicious cycle: incomplete emptying → ↑ end-expiratory volume → ↑ alveolar pressure → ↓ venous return + ↑ work to trigger. Most common in obstructive lung disease (COPD, asthma) and any time the set RR or VT outpaces what the lung can exhale.
How to fix
  • Reduce minute ventilation: ↓ RR (most effective), ↓ VT, accept hypercapnia.
  • Lengthen expiratory time: ↑ peak inspiratory flow (in VC) so Ti is shorter, or ↓ Ti (in PC).
  • Treat bronchospasm: aggressive β-agonist + steroid + (in asthma) Mg, ketamine, heliox.
  • Match ~80% of total PEEP if patient is triggering against it (improves trigger work).
  • If hemodynamic collapse: disconnect the circuit and let the chest empty — diagnostic and therapeutic.
Key takeaways
  • Auto-PEEP (intrinsic PEEP) occurs when expiratory flow doesn't return to zero before the next breath — causes increased work of triggering, hemodynamic compromise, underestimation of mechanics.
  • Detection: look at flow-time trace (no return to zero), perform end-expiratory hold (total PEEP − set PEEP = auto-PEEP).
  • Fix (in order): ↓RR (most effective), ↓VT, lengthen expiratory time, treat bronchospasm, match ~80% of total PEEP with extrinsic PEEP to ease trigger work.
  • The bedside τ check: pause the flow trace at exhalation — it should reach zero before the next breath (≥ 3τ). If not, you're trapping gas.

  • Default flow trigger 1–3 L/min; pressure trigger 1–2 cmH₂O. Modern vents almost always default to flow.
  • Too sensitive (e.g., flow < 1 L/min, pressure < 0.5 cmH₂O) → auto-triggering (false breaths from circuit motion, water, cardiogenic oscillations).
  • Not sensitive enough (e.g., flow > 4–5 L/min, pressure > 3 cmH₂O) → ineffective triggering + ↑ work of breathing.
  • If switching from flow to pressure for diagnostic reasons (e.g., leak), expect higher work and slightly slower response.
  • Always check the trigger setting at every mode change and any time the patient seems to be "fighting the vent."
The trigger work of breathingThe patient must do the work to drop airway pressure (or bias flow) below threshold before the vent assists. In an obstructive patient with auto-PEEP, the diaphragm has to first overcome the auto-PEEP gradient, then reach the trigger threshold — sometimes adding 5–10 cmH₂O of inspiratory effort per breath. That's an enormous and entirely fixable load.

17.3 The five trigger-related abnormalities

Memorize these — they cover > 95% of dyssynchrony cases:

15.3.1 Ineffective triggering (wasted effort) — the most common

Patient effort generates a small dip in pressure or flow that does not cross the trigger threshold; no breath is delivered.

  • Mechanism: usually auto-PEEP — the patient must lower alveolar pressure below the set PEEP plus the auto-PEEP, then further to reach trigger threshold. May also be: insensitive trigger, weak respiratory drive (over-sedation), leak, or pressure-support set too high (over-assistance leads to apnea / late effort / wasted attempts).
  • Recognition: small notch in pressure-time scalar or upward deflection in flow-time scalar between vent breaths, with no delivered breath. The classic "missed effort."
  • Implications: dramatically ↑ work of breathing, sleep fragmentation, distress, prolonged liberation, mortality.
  • Management (in order):
    1. Reduce auto-PEEP (the highest-yield intervention): ↓ RR, ↓ VT, lengthen expiratory time (↑ peak flow in VC, or ↓ Ti in PC), treat bronchospasm, suction.
    2. Match extrinsic PEEP to ~ 75–80% of total PEEP (counterbalance) — reduces trigger work without increasing dynamic hyperinflation.
    3. Increase trigger sensitivity (lower threshold) — but only if ineffective trigger is from sensitivity, not from auto-PEEP.
    4. Lighten over-sedation if the patient is hypopneic.
    5. Reduce excessive PSV / VT if over-assistance is the cause.
Figure 15.3.1. Ineffective triggering. Flow-time and pressure-time waveforms; the arrow marks an inspiratory effort that did not generate enough negative pressure (or flow change) to cross the trigger threshold, so no ventilator breath was delivered. Common in auto-PEEP and over-sedation.
15.3.2 Auto-triggering (false triggering)

Vent delivers a breath that the patient did not initiate.

  • Mechanism: water in tubing, large air leak (cuff or circuit), cardiogenic oscillations (high stroke volume in a thin-walled chest), motion artifact, oversensitive trigger.
  • Recognition: breath delivered while patient is apneic / paralyzed / disconnected; rate higher than expected; often regular cardiac-pattern triggering at HR.
  • Implications: respiratory alkalosis, sleep disruption, barotrauma risk, false weaning data.
  • Management: empty water traps; check ETT cuff and circuit for leak; raise trigger threshold (less sensitive); switch flow → pressure trigger if cardiogenic; stabilize the circuit.
Figure 15.3.2a. Auto-triggering. The ventilator delivers a positive-pressure breath without patient initiation. Look for breath rate higher than expected and (when the patient is paralyzed/disconnected) breaths still being delivered.
Figure 15.3.2b. The three classic causes of auto-triggering. Left: circuit condensation — sawtooth oscillations on the flow trace from water moving in the tubing. Center: cardiac oscillations — small regular oscillations synchronized with heartbeat. Right: circuit leak — expired tidal volume (Vte) is less than inspired (Vti) and volume scalar fails to return to zero. Each has a different fix (drain water / switch to pressure trigger / find and seal the leak).
15.3.3 Double triggering (breath stacking)

Two ventilator breaths back-to-back with no exhalation between, producing a single very large effective tidal volume.

  • Mechanism: the patient's neural inspiratory time exceeds the vent's set Ti (or set VT); when the vent cycles off, the patient's continuing effort triggers a second breath. Classic in awake ARDS on a 6-mL/kg low-VT setting; also in pain, anxiety, fever, light sedation.
  • Recognition: two pressure peaks back-to-back with no return to PEEP; volume curve shows stacked rise; alarms for high pressure may sound.
  • Implications: delivered VT can double — e.g., 12 mL/kg PBW in an ARDS patient — directly causing volutrauma and P-SILI. Strongly correlated with mortality.24
  • Management (escalating):
    1. Match the set Ti to the patient's neural Ti (e.g., extend from 0.7 → 1.0 s).
    2. If VT can be increased without violating Pplat ≤ 30 / ΔP ≤ 15, do so.
    3. Switch from VC to PC (decelerating flow more comfortable; pressure ceiling).
    4. Treat pain (fentanyl) and anxiety; deepen sedation to RASS −2 to −3.
    5. If severe ARDS with persistent stacking despite optimization → NMB (cisatracurium).
Figure 15.3.3. Double triggering. Two ventilator breaths delivered back-to-back with no expiration in between. The arrow marks the second stacked breath; the resulting effective tidal volume can be 2× the set VT — direct volutrauma in ARDS.
15.3.4 Reverse triggering (entrainment)

Diaphragm contracts after a passive ventilator breath; the respiratory center synchronizes ("entrains") to the machine rhythm. Increasingly recognized — found in > 50% of deeply sedated ARDS patients on continuous monitoring.

  • Mechanism: brainstem entrainment in deeply sedated but not paralyzed patients; the lung-stretch from the vent breath provokes a delayed reflex inspiration.
  • Recognition: small effort visible on flow or pressure scalar after the start of the machine breath (different from ineffective trigger, which is between breaths). Esophageal manometry confirms. May "drag" a second breath (reverse-triggered double-trigger), causing pendelluft (gas redistribution within the lung) and regional volutrauma.
  • Implications: P-SILI, asymmetric stress in heterogeneous ARDS lungs, complicates mechanics interpretation.
  • Management: often paradoxical — lightening sedation may eliminate it (so the patient breathes spontaneously rather than entrains); alternatively, deepening sedation with NMB. Evidence is evolving; treat case-by-case.
15.3.5 Trigger delay

Time between patient effort and vent delivery exceeds 100 ms (target < 50 ms).

  • Mechanism: insensitive trigger, high airway resistance, high circuit resistance, auto-PEEP, very low respiratory drive.
  • Recognition: visible offset on simultaneous diaphragm-electrical/airway-pressure tracings; patient distress and "out-of-phase" appearance.
  • Implications: ↑ inspiratory work, fatigue, weaning failure.
  • Management: switch pressure → flow trigger; lower trigger threshold; reduce auto-PEEP; suction; consider NAVA in refractory cases.

17.4 Flow asynchrony ("flow starvation")

The set ventilator flow rate is lower than the patient's desired flow rate. The patient generates additional negative inspiratory pressure to "pull" more flow — visible as concave deflections on the pressure-time scalar.

  • Recognition: concave ("scooped") inspiratory pressure trace; sometimes flow trace shows a peak below the patient's perceived demand.
  • Causes: inadequate set flow (most common in VC with square waveform), high respiratory drive (pain, dyspnea, fever, delirium), small ETT.
  • Fix: ↑ peak flow in VC (e.g., 60 → 80 L/min); switch square → decelerating flow; ↑ inspiratory pressure in PC/PSV; treat the underlying drive (analgesia, anxiolysis, treat fever).
Figure 15.4. Flow dyssynchrony. Set flow is too low for patient demand; the patient generates negative inspiratory pressure to recruit more flow, producing concave deflections in the pressure tracing (arrows). Increasing ventilator flow typically resolves it.

17.5 Cycle dyssynchrony — premature cycling

The ventilator ends inspiration before the patient does. The airway pressure trace is pulled downward at end-inspiration, and reversal of expiratory flow occurs as the patient continues to inhale.

  • Causes: set Ti too short (PC, VC); cycle threshold set too high in PSV (e.g., 40% in a stiff lung that wants 15%); high expiratory time constant (COPD).
  • Risk: often progresses to double-triggering (the unsatisfied effort triggers a second breath).
  • Fix: ↑ Ti in PC; ↑ VT in VC; lower the expiratory cycling threshold in PSV (e.g., 25% → 10–15% in COPD).
Figure 15.5. Premature cycling (cycle dyssynchrony — early termination). The ventilator ends inspiration before the patient does; the pressure curve is pulled downward and a brief reversal of expiratory flow appears as the patient continues to pull air in.

17.6 Cycle dyssynchrony — delayed cycling

The ventilator stays in inspiration after the patient wants to exhale. The patient exhales against an inflating breath, producing a terminal pressure spike and increased expiratory flow when the vent finally cycles.

  • Causes: set Ti too long (PC, VC); cycle threshold set too low in PSV (e.g., 10% in a stiff lung wants 30–40%); long inspiratory time constant (ARDS).
  • Fix: ↓ Ti in PC; raise the expiratory cycling threshold in PSV (e.g., 25% → 35–40% in stiff ARDS lungs); switch from time-cycled to flow-cycled mode.
Figure 15.6. Delayed cycling (cycle dyssynchrony — delayed termination). Green arrows on the flow trace show expiratory flow variation; blue arrows on the pressure trace show end-inspiratory pressure spikes from the patient's expiratory effort against the still-open vent.

17.7 Late-cycle dyssynchrony — pressure control vs volume control

The pattern of late-cycle dyssynchrony differs by mode. In pressure control, the patient's neural inspiratory time can be longer than the vent's set Ti; pressure remains constant but the patient's effort continues. In volume control, the vent continues delivering volume even after the patient wants to exhale, producing pressure elevation as the patient pushes against the still-inflating breath.

Figure 15.7. Late-cycle dyssynchrony in PC (left) vs VC (right). Both show the patient's neural inspiratory time (It) ending before the vent's set inspiratory time. Optimal synchrony in VC-CMV is achieved by directly shortening Ti or increasing flow; in PC-CMV by decreasing Ti to match the patient's neural Ti.

17.8 Early-cycle dyssynchrony — detail by mode

Early termination of the vent breath while the patient is still inhaling. Expressed differently in three settings: VC with decelerating flow, VC with square (constant) flow, and PC.

Figure 15.8. Early-cycle dyssynchrony in three modes. Top: VC, decelerating ramp — flow falls before patient effort ends. Middle: VC, square waveform — abrupt termination with continued patient effort visible on the Pmus trace. Bottom: PC mode — early opening of the expiratory valve before the patient effort is complete; the patient may attempt to inhale during exhalation, producing a double-trigger.

17.9 The asynchrony index — quantifying the burden

$$\text{AI} \;=\; \frac{\text{asynchronous breaths}}{\text{total breaths}} \times 100\%$$
  • AI is calculated over a short observation window (typically 100 breaths or 15 minutes).
  • AI > 10% is the threshold associated with prolonged ventilation, longer ICU stay, and higher in-hospital and 1-year mortality.24
  • Ineffective triggering specifically: > 10 events/hour predicts weaning failure.6
  • Most ICUs do not measure AI routinely — but eyeballing the screen for > 10% wasted/double/late efforts during a 1-minute observation is a powerful clinical surrogate.
15.9.1 Worked example — calculating AI at the bedside in one minute

Scenario: 64M with COPD exacerbation on PSV 12/PEEP 8. You watch the pressure-time scalar for 60 seconds and record:

  • Total breaths attempted (counting both vent-delivered breaths and visible patient efforts that didn't trigger): 22
  • Vent-delivered breaths: 16 (pressure rises to PSV plateau)
  • Failed efforts (small pressure dips that don't reach trigger): 5 — ineffective triggering
  • Double-trigger events (back-to-back pressure peaks without exhalation): 1

Calculation:

$$\begin{aligned} \text{Asynchronous breaths} &= 5\,(\text{ineffective}) + 1\,(\text{double}) = 6 \\ \text{Total breaths attempted} &= 22 \\ \text{AI} &= \frac{6}{22} \times 100 = \mathbf{27\%} \end{aligned}$$

Interpretation: AI 27% is well above the 10% mortality threshold. Ineffective triggering is the dominant pattern (5 of 6 events) — almost certainly auto-PEEP given COPD physiology. Confirm with end-expiratory pause; treat by lowering RR/VT, lengthening Te, and applying extrinsic PEEP at ~ 75–80% of measured auto-PEEP. Recheck AI in 15–30 min after each adjustment; the goal is < 10%.

The 60-second AI assessmentYou don't need a fancy monitor. Set a stopwatch, watch one minute of the pressure waveform, count vent breaths and add visible failed efforts and stacked breaths to the denominator. AI > 10% in any single minute predicts the same outcomes as the formal 100-breath / 15-min window in research studies.

17.10 Bedside management algorithm — putting it together

  1. Recognize — pause at the bedside and watch one minute of three waveforms (P-time, F-time, V-time). Eyeball asynchrony rate.
  2. Classify — is the issue at trigger, flow, or cycle? (Most asynchrony is trigger-related.)
  3. Diagnose the mechanism — auto-PEEP? Sedation level? Mode mismatch? Leak? Pain / fever? Underlying disease?
  4. Fix the patient first — analgesia, anxiolysis, treat underlying disease (bronchospasm, fever, agitation).
  5. Fix the ventilator — sensitivity, mode, VT, Ti, peak flow, PEEP, cycling threshold.
  6. Reassess after each change — never make multiple ventilator changes simultaneously.
  7. If refractory in severe ARDS — consider NMB (cisatracurium) or NAVA / PAV+ at experienced centers.
Three rules of thumb(1) The most common asynchrony is ineffective triggering from auto-PEEP — fix the auto-PEEP, not the trigger. (2) The most dangerous asynchrony is double-triggering in ARDS — fix it before the patient barotraumatizes themselves. (3) The most under-diagnosed asynchrony is reverse triggering — esophageal manometry confirms when the diagnosis matters.
Key takeaways
  • Triggering is the single most clinically consequential ventilator interface — bad trigger setup creates work, distress, sleep loss, P-SILI, and increased mortality.
  • Three systems: pressure trigger (~50 ms latency, foiled by leaks), flow trigger (~2 L/min, faster, modern default), neural trigger (NAVA, zero latency, requires EMG catheter).
  • Five major asynchrony types: ineffective triggering (auto-PEEP patient can't reach threshold), auto-triggering (circuit water/leak), double-triggering (patient effort exceeds vent cycle), flow starvation, reverse triggering.
  • The most common is ineffective triggering from auto-PEEP — fix the auto-PEEP, not the trigger sensitivity.

Point-of-care ultrasound has moved from "nice to have" to "expected" in the modern ICU. Six focused exams answer most ventilator-related bedside questions in under 10 minutes — diaphragm function, lung consolidation/edema, pneumothorax, pleural effusion, fluid responsiveness, and cardiac structure/function. Each is fast, learnable, and changes management more often than the test it replaces.

18.3 Diaphragm ultrasound — thickening fraction (TF) and excursion

Diaphragm ultrasound at the zone of apposition (mid-axillary line, 8th–10th intercostal space, B-mode) measures diaphragm thickness at end-expiration and end-inspiration:

$$\text{TF} \;=\; \frac{T_{\text{end-insp}} - T_{\text{end-exp}}}{T_{\text{end-exp}}} \times 100\%$$
  • TF > 30%: normal diaphragm function. Predicts SBT success and successful extubation.
  • TF 15–30%: borderline; weaning may be slow.
  • TF < 15%: diaphragm dysfunction — paralysis, severe weakness, or excessive ventilator support that has unloaded the diaphragm. Strongly predicts extubation failure.35

Diaphragm excursion (M-mode at subcostal view) is an alternative measure: > 1.0–1.5 cm during quiet breathing is normal; < 1.0 cm in spontaneous breathing predicts weaning failure. Excursion is easier to obtain but excursion-based criteria are less specific than TF for diaphragm function (because excursion is influenced by lung volume, position, and effort).

Why TF beats excursion in vented patientsExcursion can be entirely passive (the vent is doing the work). TF reflects active muscular contraction of the diaphragm itself — a direct measure of what the patient's pump can do.

18.4 Pleural effusion quantification

Effusion appears as anechoic (black) collection at the dependent base, with the diaphragm and atelectatic lung visible above. Quantification methods:

  • Balik formula: estimated volume (mL) = inter-pleural distance (mm at end-expiration) × 20.
  • Effusion > 50 mm depth at the lung base = ~1000 mL — drainage usually high-yield for oxygenation.
  • "Plankton sign" (echogenic particles floating) suggests exudate or hemothorax; static anechoic suggests transudate.

Bedside drainage with US guidance is far safer than landmark-based; reduces PTX rate from ~6% to ~1%.

Figure 15b.4 — The Balik method for quantifying pleural effusion volume. With the patient supine, the probe is placed at the posterior axillary line over the dependent hemithorax. The maximum perpendicular distance between the parietal and visceral pleural surfaces (the inter-pleural separation) is measured at end-expiration. Estimated volume (mL) ≈ inter-pleural distance (mm) × 20. An inter-pleural distance of 50 mm therefore predicts ~1000 mL of fluid — usually high-yield for thoracentesis, with measurable improvement in oxygenation and compliance after drainage.

18.5 IVC ultrasound for fluid responsiveness — caveats

Inferior vena cava (IVC) measured 2 cm distal to the cavoatrial junction in subxiphoid view, M-mode through the long axis:

  • Spontaneously breathing: IVC inspiratory collapse > 50% suggests fluid responsiveness; < 25% suggests not.
  • Mechanically ventilated, passive: "distensibility index" — IVCmax − IVCmin / IVCmin × 100. > 18% suggests fluid-responsive.
  • Caveats: any spontaneous effort, arrhythmia, RV failure, abdominal hypertension, or open chest invalidates these. False positives in pulmonary HTN are common.
The IVC fallacyIVC variability has poor positive predictive value when used in isolation. Pair with passive leg raise (PLR) test — a more reliable bedside maneuver: lift legs 45° for 1 minute and look for ≥ 10% rise in stroke volume / cardiac output (echo or pulse contour). PLR works regardless of vent mode or rhythm.

18.6 Focused cardiac ultrasound — the four views every intensivist should know

  • Parasternal long axis (PLAX): LV size and gross function, septal motion, pericardial effusion, mitral/aortic valves.
  • Parasternal short axis (PSAX) at papillary level: LV global function, RV size relative to LV, septal flattening (RV pressure overload — "D-sign").
  • Apical four-chamber (A4C): RV/LV size and function, McConnell's sign (RV free-wall hypokinesis with apical sparing — classic for acute PE), tricuspid regurgitation jet for PASP.
  • Subxiphoid: pericardial effusion, IVC, tamponade physiology.

Five questions cardiac POCUS answers in < 5 minutes:

  1. Is the LV severely depressed? → consider inotrope, treat as cardiogenic shock.
  2. Is the RV dilated/dysfunctional? → think PE, RV-protective vent, avoid fluids.
  3. Is there a pericardial effusion or tamponade? → drain.
  4. Is the IVC plethoric and non-collapsible? → not fluid-responsive.
  5. Is there severe valvular disease? → context for shock or edema.

18.9 Fluid responsiveness — the framework

The question "does this patient need fluid?" hinges on where they sit on the Frank-Starling curve. Static indicators (CVP, PAWP, LVEDP) tell you filling pressure, not preload. A patient with CVP 15 may be on the flat part of the curve — additional fluid will raise pressure but not increase cardiac output. Dynamic indicators ask the right question instead: "Will the next bolus increase stroke volume?"

The physiology: positive-pressure breaths cyclically reduce preload (intrathoracic pressure squeezes the great veins, reducing LV end-diastolic volume). On the steep part of the Starling curve, stroke volume oscillates with the cycle. On the flat part, it doesn't. Dynamic tests exploit this.

One sentence: Static filling pressures are dead. Ask instead: "Does the ventilator breath make stroke volume dance?" If yes — fluid responsive.

18.10 Pulse pressure variation (PPV) & stroke volume variation (SVV)

Physiology. A positive-pressure breath ↓ preload → ↓ SV → ↓ pulse pressure. The amplitude of cyclic pulse-pressure swing across a breath, normalized to mean pulse pressure, is PPV.

$$\text{PPV} \;=\; \frac{PP_{\max} - PP_{\min}}{(PP_{\max} + PP_{\min})/2} \times 100\%$$
$$\text{SVV} \;=\; \frac{SV_{\max} - SV_{\min}}{SV_{\text{mean}}} \times 100\%$$

Threshold. PPV > 13% (or SVV > 10–12%) predicts fluid responsiveness with sens/spec ~ 85% — the workhorse dynamic test in the controlled-mode ICU patient.

Critical caveats — PPV/SVV is unreliable when:

  • Spontaneous effort. Patient-triggered breaths replace machine-driven cyclic preload swings.
  • Arrhythmia (especially Afib). Beat-to-beat variability swamps the ventilator-induced signal.
  • Tidal volume < 8 mL/kg PBW. Cyclic pressure swing too small to generate measurable PPV.
  • RV failure. RV preload-limited; cyclic SV change is dampened.
  • Open chest, intra-abdominal hypertension, low chest-wall compliance. Pleural pressure transmission unpredictable.

Tidal-volume challenge. If VT is the limiting factor, briefly increase from 6 → 8 mL/kg PBW for 1 minute. If PPV rises ≥ 3.5%, the patient is genuinely responsive.

Common mistakeTrusting PPV > 13% in a patient breathing spontaneously, in fast Afib, or on VT 5 mL/kg. The test is valid only under controlled-mode, regular-rhythm, VT ≥ 8 conditions.

18.11 Passive leg raise (PLR) — the universal bedside test

Mechanism. PLR is a reversible autotransfusion: lifting the legs from supine displaces ~ 250–300 mL of venous blood centrally, transiently raising preload without committing fluid.

Method. Start semi-recumbent at 45°; lower torso to flat; lift both legs to 45° for exactly 1 minute (no longer). Measure SV or CO immediately before and during the maneuver.

Measurement. Echo LVOT VTI, arterial pulse-contour analysis, or non-invasive CO monitor. Threshold: SV/CO ↑ ≥ 10% during PLR = fluid responsive.

The test that always worksPLR is independent of ventilation mode, rhythm, and tidal volume because it doesn't rely on ventilator mechanics — it relies on physiology. Spontaneous breathing? Works. Afib? Works. VT 5 mL/kg? Still works.

Limitations. Significant leg edema, recent vascular surgery, orthopedic fixation, severe agitation. Otherwise feasible in nearly every ventilated patient.

18.12 End-expiratory occlusion test (EEO)

Hold expiration for 15 seconds → no positive-pressure breaths → cyclic preload reduction abolished → preload increases acutely. Threshold: SV/CO ↑ ≥ 5% = fluid responsive.

Method. Measure baseline SV/CO; occlude expiratory port (or hit "expiratory hold") for 15 s; release and measure again immediately.

Use when: PLR contraindicated; quick test on a stable patient with reasonable oxygenation reserve. Avoid when: marginal SpO₂ or high work of breathing — 15 s without a breath may not be tolerated.

18.13 Mini-fluid challenge

If PPV/SVV, PLR, and EEO are all inconclusive or unavailable: 100 mL crystalloid IV over 1 min, recheck SV/CO at 5 min. Rise ≥ 6% = responsive. Smaller and safer than the traditional 500 mL bolus while still informative.

18.14 Putting it together — fluid responsiveness algorithm

  1. Conditions right for PPV/SVV? Regular rhythm, VT ≥ 8, no spontaneous effort, no RV failure or IAH.
    • Yes → PPV (cutoff 13%) or SVV (cutoff 12%).
    • No → step 2.
  2. PLR feasible? Legs available, no vascular/orthopedic contraindication, SV/CO measurement available.
    • Yes → PLR (cutoff +10% SV/CO).
    • No → step 3.
  3. EEO tolerable? Patient stable enough for 15-s breath hold.
    • Yes → EEO (cutoff +5% SV/CO).
    • No → step 4.
  4. Mini-bolus 100 mL over 1 min; reassess at 5 min (cutoff +6%).

Once you've identified responsiveness, then decide volume. A 250–500 mL bolus is a reasonable starting point; reassess at 30 min before giving more.

Key takeaways
  • BLUE protocol: A-lines (normal aerated lung), B-lines (≥3 per space = interstitial syndrome), lung sliding (present = exclude PTX), lung point (100% specific for PTX).
  • Diaphragm thickening fraction (TF) > 30% predicts SBT and extubation success; < 15% strongly predicts failure; more specific than excursion alone.
  • IVC variability has poor positive predictive value in isolation — pair with passive leg raise (PLR) for reliable fluid-responsiveness assessment.
  • Focused cardiac POCUS (PLAX, PSAX, A4C, subxiphoid) answers five critical questions about LV, RV, pericardium, and tamponade within 5 minutes.
  • Static filling pressures (CVP, PAWP) do not predict fluid responsiveness — abandon them as primary tools.
  • PPV > 13% / SVV > 10–12% predict responsiveness only under strict conditions: controlled mode, regular rhythm, VT ≥ 8 mL/kg PBW, no spontaneous effort.
  • PLR is the universal test — works regardless of mode, rhythm, or VT because it relies on physiology, not mechanics.
  • Algorithm: PPV/SVV (if conditions met) → PLR → EEO → mini-bolus.
Part IV

Disease-specific strategies

The same vent in a different patient becomes a different tool. The five archetypes below cover > 95% of ICU work.

19ARDS

ARDS is the prototypical critical-care vent challenge. Mortality has been driven down by lung-protective ventilation, prone positioning, conservative fluid balance, and avoiding ventilator-induced injury — not by any specific pharmacology. The strategy is mechanical, and the strategy is the patient's outcome.

19.1 Definition & phenotypes

Berlin criteria (2012): acute (< 1 wk) bilateral opacities not fully explained by cardiac failure, with hypoxemia on PEEP ≥ 5: mild (P/F 200–300), moderate (100–200), severe (< 100).7

Global Definition of ARDS (2023): expands to include patients on HFNC ≥ 30 L/min and uses SpO₂/FiO₂ ratio when blood gas not available; recognizes ARDS in resource-limited settings.1

Pathophysiology — diffuse alveolar damage (DAD). Three sequential phases: exudative (0–7 d, capillary leak + neutrophil influx + hyaline membranes), proliferative (7–21 d, type-II pneumocyte hyperplasia, fibroblast activation), fibrotic (after ~3 wk, collagen deposition; the cause of long-term restrictive deficit).30 The exudative phase explains the shunt physiology and edema; the proliferative phase explains why the lung becomes harder to recruit; the fibrotic phase explains chronic morbidity.

  1. VT 4–6 mL/kg PBW; lower if Pplat > 30. The ARMA trial (ARDS Network low-tidal-volume study) showed 22% relative mortality reduction.8

19.5 Prone positioning — the protocol

  1. Pre-prone: hold tube feeds 1 h prior, secure ETT and lines, eye protection, padding (forehead, chin, shoulders, knees, dorsal feet), 5-person team minimum.
  2. Maneuver: sheet-roll technique; rotate 180°; position arms in "swimmer's" pose; reposition every 2 h to prevent pressure injury.
  3. Vent adjustments: often PEEP can be reduced 2–4 cmH₂O after first 1–2 h as oxygenation improves; recheck Pplat and ΔP at 30 min, 1 h, 4 h.
  4. Duration: 16+ h/day (PROSEVA used 17 ± 3 h); supinate AM for procedures, lines, CT, then re-prone.
  5. Stopping criteria: P/F > 150 supine on PEEP ≤ 10 and FiO₂ ≤ 0.6 sustained ≥ 4 h; OR no response after 4 sessions; OR life-threatening complication (extubation, arrest in prone).
The proning catastropheThe two emergencies during prone are unplanned extubation and cardiac arrest. Pre-position a "supinate immediately" plan with the team; mark the bed-rail emergency role. Have a pre-prepared replacement ETT and difficult-airway cart at bedside.

19.6 NMB in ARDS — practical use

  • Cisatracurium (Hofmann elimination, no organ dependence) is the historic first-line: 15–20 mg bolus, then 37.5–75 mg/h infusion. Reassess at 24 h, daily after.
  • Rocuronium (cheaper, hepatic clearance) acceptable; less ideal in liver failure.
  • Monitor with train-of-four (TOF) at the orbicularis oculi or adductor pollicis; target 1–2 of 4 twitches.
  • Adjuncts always: deep sedation (RASS −5), eye care (lubrication + tape), DVT prophylaxis, daily reassessment for discontinuation.
  • Risks: ICU-acquired weakness (myopathy especially with concurrent corticosteroids), nerve injury from positioning.

19.7 Inhaled pulmonary vasodilators

  • iNO 5–20 ppm — selective pulmonary vasodilator improves V/Q matching by preferentially dilating vessels in ventilated lung regions. Methemoglobinemia & rebound pulmonary HTN on abrupt withdrawal are the major risks. Cost is significant.
  • Inhaled epoprostenol 30–50 ng/kg/min — similar physiology, much cheaper, no methemoglobinemia. Sticky on circuits; change vent circuit q24 h.
  • Decision: start one if P/F < 100 refractory; titrate to oxygenation; wean within 48–72 h to avoid rebound. Document failure to respond and de-escalate.
  • Caveat: no mortality benefit in pooled trials; bridge therapy only.

19.8 ECMO criteria — when to call

Call EARLY for transport considerations. EOLIA enrollment criteria define the high-bar candidate:13

  • P/F < 50 for > 3 h, OR
  • P/F < 80 for > 6 h, OR
  • pH < 7.25 with PaCO₂ ≥ 60 for > 6 h, with RR 35 and Pplat ≤ 32
  • Despite optimized vent: PEEP ≥ 10, NMB, prone trialed (or contraindicated), iNO trialed
  • Reversible underlying cause; < 7 days of high-pressure vent (the longer the vent, the worse the outcome)
  • No major contraindication (irreversible neurologic injury, advanced metastatic disease, multi-organ failure beyond lungs)

VV-ECMO allows ultra-protective ventilation (VT 3–4 mL/kg, ΔP < 12, RR 4–10) — the lung is "rested" while healing. VA-ECMO is reserved for combined cardiac and respiratory failure (e.g., massive PE with shock).

Why early mattersOutcomes after ECMO in ARDS correlate with the duration of pre-ECMO mechanical ventilation. Each day on high pressures before cannulation is a step closer to fibroproliferative ARDS, where the lung cannot recover even off the ventilator.

19.9 Adjuncts that don't (clearly) help — and a few that do

  • Corticosteroids: methylprednisolone 1 mg/kg/day (DEXA-ARDS regimen showed mortality benefit in early moderate-severe ARDS in 2020); dexamethasone 6 mg in COVID-ARDS (RECOVERY). Reasonable in early ARDS without contraindication.
  • Conservative fluids: FACTT showed more vent-free days with conservative balance after shock resolved; aim for even-to-negative balance once perfusion adequate.12
  • Higher PEEP only when recruitable: not for everyone (ART trial cautioned against routine recruitment maneuvers).16
  • HFOV: harm signal (OSCAR/OSCILLATE); abandoned for adult ARDS.
  • Surfactant, β-agonists, statins, omega-3s: no benefit in ARDS.

19.10 The 5-element protective bundle (Hess)

Memorize this as the "every-time" check:26

  1. VT 4–8 mL/kg PBW (start 6, lower if Pplat > 30)
  2. Pplat ≤ 28 cmH₂O (some say 30; Hess targets 28)
  3. Driving pressure ≤ 15 cmH₂O
  4. PEEP per phenotype + table
  5. FiO₂ for SpO₂ 88–95%
Key takeaways
  • VT 4–6 mL/kg PBW, Pplat ≤ 30, ΔP ≤ 15. These three numbers carry most of the mortality benefit.
  • Phenotype matters: focal/lobar ARDS overdistends with high PEEP; diffuse ARDS recruits with high PEEP. Pair the FiO₂ ladder with imaging.
  • Owens rescue ladder (one rung at a time): basics → sedation/NMB → PEEP → prone → NMB infusion → iNO → ECMO. Re-evaluate at 4 h between steps.
  • Prone ≥ 16 h/day for P/F < 150 (PROSEVA): 16% absolute mortality reduction — largest single ICU intervention effect.
  • Routine NMB no longer recommended (ROSE); reserve for refractory dyssynchrony or ΔP > 15 despite optimization.
  • EOLIA criteria + early ECMO referral if P/F < 80 sustained or refractory hypercapnia. Don't delay — outcomes worsen with each day on high pressures pre-cannulation.

20Asthma & COPD — the obstructive playbook

The dominant problem is dynamic hyperinflation from a long expiratory time constant (τ = R × C) that the patient can't fully empty before the next breath. The vent strategy in one phrase: let them exhale. The hemodynamic strategy in one phrase: volume first, then sedation, then bronchodilation.27

20.1 The pathophysiology you must hold in mind

  • Asthma: bronchospasm + airway edema + mucus plugging → expiratory flow limitation. Reversible component dominates. Lungs are otherwise normal.
  • COPD: emphysematous loss of elastic recoil + small-airway disease + dynamic compression on exhalation → expiratory flow limitation. Less reversible. Often baseline hypercapnic and hypoxemic.
  • The shared physiology: incomplete exhalation → end-expiratory volume above FRC → intrinsic PEEP (auto-PEEP) → inspiratory threshold load (the patient must generate enough negative pressure to overcome auto-PEEP before the vent can sense effort) → trigger asynchrony, missed breaths, dyspnea, exhaustion.

20.3 Auto-PEEP — measure it, manage it

Measure: end-expiratory pause (3–5 s) on a passive breath. Total PEEP − set PEEP = auto-PEEP. (See §16 for the technique and pitfalls.)

Manage (in order):

  1. Lengthen expiratory time: ↓ RR, ↑ peak inspiratory flow, ↓ Ti, ↓ VT.
  2. Treat the airway: bronchodilators, steroids, treat infection.
  3. Apply PEEP to overcome inspiratory threshold load: set PEEP at ~75–85% of measured auto-PEEP. Counter-intuitive but life-changing — it doesn't add to total PEEP; it just reduces the patient's work to trigger.
  4. If hemodynamic compromise: disconnect from vent, let chest empty (5–10 s of apnea), give IV fluids, then reconnect with reduced minute ventilation.

  1. Standard: continuous albuterol + ipratropium nebs, IV methylpred 60–125 mg, IV Mg 2 g.
  2. Intubation triggers: silent chest, exhaustion, altered mental status, rising PaCO₂ despite max therapy. Use ketamine for induction (1.5 mg/kg) + rocuronium; ketamine bronchodilates. Avoid hypotension-prone agents.
  3. On vent: Owens recipe above; deep sedation, often NMB short-term.
  4. Refractory: heliox 70:30 (reduces airway resistance — see §20.5a).
  5. Ketamine drip (1–3 mg/kg/h) — bronchodilation + sedation in one agent.
  6. Inhalational anesthetic (sevoflurane, isoflurane) at experienced centers — direct smooth-muscle relaxation.
  7. VV-ECMO if refractory hypercapnia (pH < 7.10) despite all of the above.
17.5a Heliox — the density-reduction trick

Heliox is a helium–oxygen mixture (typically 70:30 or 80:20 helium:O₂). Helium is far less dense than nitrogen (~ ⅓ the density at the same FiO₂). Why does this help in obstruction?

  • Reynolds number drops — turbulent flow through narrowed airways becomes laminar. Resistance to flow falls; less pressure is needed for the same flow.
  • Time constant shortens — τ = R × C; lower R means faster filling and emptying of obstructed lung units.
  • Better drug distribution — nebulized particles travel further into peripheral airways with helium as the carrier gas.

Practical caveats:

  • Helium has no bronchodilator effect of its own; it just makes flow easier while waiting for steroids and bronchodilators to work.
  • FiO₂ ceiling of 30–40% — patients needing higher FiO₂ can't use heliox effectively (you can't have helium and oxygen above ~ 70% concentration).
  • Many ventilators are not calibrated for heliox — flow and volume readings can be off by 20–40%. Check institutional compatibility before use.
  • Outcome data are mixed; heliox is a bridge to definitive therapy, not a treatment.

20.6 COPD — the NIV-first imperative

COPD exacerbations with hypercapnic respiratory failure (pH < 7.35, PaCO₂ > 45) and intact mental status / airway should receive NIV (BiPAP) before intubation. NIV reduces intubation rate by ~50% and mortality by ~40% in this population (multiple RCTs, Cochrane). Set IPAP 10–14, EPAP 4–6, titrate IPAP up by 2 q5 min for tidal volume of 6–8 mL/kg PBW. Reassess at 1 h: pH up, RR down, encephalopathy improving = winning. If not, intubate.

The hyperoxia trap in COPDThe classic teaching that "100% O₂ removes hypoxic drive and causes CO₂ retention" overstates the mechanism — but the observation is real. The actual mechanism is mostly Haldane effect (oxygenated Hb binds less CO₂, raising PaCO₂) plus V/Q redistribution (loss of hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction → more dead space). Either way: in COPD, target SpO₂ 88–92%, not 100%.
Key takeaways
  • Dominant problem: dynamic hyperinflation from long expiratory time constant. Strategy in one phrase: let them exhale.
  • Owens recipe for severe asthma: VT 6 mL/kg PBW, RR 8–10, I:E 1:4–1:5, peak flow 80–100, square waveform, PEEP 0–5, deep sedation, permissive hypercapnia (pH ≥ 7.15).
  • Sudden hypotension on the vent → DOPE-A. The 30-second test: disconnect the circuit. BP recovers in 5–10 s = auto-PEEP confirmed.
  • Apply extrinsic PEEP at 75–85% of measured auto-PEEP — reduces trigger work without adding hyperinflation.
  • COPD: NIV first if pH < 7.35 with intact mental status — reduces intubation by 50%, mortality by 40%. Reassess at 1 h.
  • Target SpO₂ 88–92% in COPD (avoid hyperoxia). Target PaCO₂ at the patient's baseline, not 40.

21Cardiogenic pulmonary edema

The ventilator in cardiogenic edema is doing two things at once: oxygenating across flooded alveoli and unloading the failing LV. The hemodynamic effect is often more important than the gas-exchange effect. Get the strategy right and most patients are extubated within 24–48 hours; get it wrong and you turn a treatable edema into prolonged ARDS-style ventilation.

21.1 The two physiologic effects of positive pressure on the LV

  • ↓ Preload: positive intrathoracic pressure compresses the great veins, reducing venous return to the right heart and thereby pulmonary venous return to the left heart. This is the same mechanism that causes hypotension after intubation in hypovolemia — but in pulmonary edema it is therapeutic.
  • ↓ LV afterload: LV transmural pressure = LV cavity pressure − pleural pressure. When pleural pressure rises during positive-pressure inspiration, LV transmural pressure falls — the LV "feels" less wall tension and ejects more easily. This effect is the dominant mechanism behind NIV's dramatic improvement in cardiogenic edema (often within 30 min).
The reverse is also true: a large negative inspiratory effort (anxious patient, dyspnea, nasal flaring) raises LV transmural pressure — adding afterload to an already failing heart. This is why the labored, distressed pre-intubation cardiogenic-edema patient often crashes during induction even before the vent settings matter — the loss of negative-pressure swings should reduce afterload, but vasoplegia from sedation drops SVR faster.

21.2 NIV first — the evidence

Multiple RCTs and meta-analyses demonstrate that CPAP or BiPAP reduces intubation by 40% and mortality by 20% in acute cardiogenic edema vs standard O₂. Initial settings:

  • CPAP 8–12 cmH₂O + FiO₂ titrated to SpO₂ > 92%. Equivalent outcomes to BiPAP for pure cardiogenic edema; technically simpler.
  • BiPAP IPAP 10–14 / EPAP 5–8 — preferred if hypercapnic (mixed COPD/CHF) or if work-of-breathing is a major component.
  • Reassess at 1 h: SpO₂, RR, mental status, BP. Improvement → continue and diurese. Failure (HACOR > 5, falling pH, deteriorating mental status) → intubate.

21.4 The post-extubation flash-edema risk

Marginal LV function patients can develop pulmonary edema within minutes-hours of extubation as the swing back to negative-pressure inspiration raises LV afterload abruptly. Risk highest in: EF < 35%, severe MR/AS, prior CHF admission, positive fluid balance > 3 L during ICU stay, ischemic cardiomyopathy.

Prevention: aggressive diuresis pre-extubation (target net negative or even); plan for prophylactic post-extubation NIV (BiPAP IPAP 10/EPAP 5) for the first 24 h in high-risk patients; have nitroglycerine and BiPAP set up at the bedside.

The "wet then dry" trickIf the post-extubation patient becomes hypoxic with B-lines on lung US within 4 hours, NIV + IV bolus diuretic is usually faster and more effective than re-intubation. Reserve re-intubation for failure of NIV at 1–2 h.

21.5 Common pitfalls

  • Underdiuresing because BP is "borderline": cardiogenic edema usually has elevated filling pressures with normal-to-low BP. The right answer is afterload reduction (nitroprusside) + inotrope (dobutamine) + diuresis — not just diuresis hold.
  • Mistaking flash edema for ARDS: B-line score on US, BNP/NT-proBNP, echo LV function, and rapid response to diuretic differentiate. ARDS does not improve with diuretic alone.
  • Withholding NIV because of "altered mentation": severe hypercapnic CHF often has CO₂-induced encephalopathy that improves with NIV in 30 min. If you can support the airway briefly, a NIV trial is reasonable; intubate if not improving in 1 h.
Key takeaways
  • Cardiogenic edema: positive pressure ↓ preload (compression of great veins) AND ↓ LV afterload (↑ pleural pressure = ↓ LV transmural pressure) — both therapeutic.
  • NIV first (CPAP 8–12 or BiPAP 10/5): reduces intubation 40% and mortality 20% — reassess at 1 h with HACOR score or HFNC criteria.
  • If intubated: PEEP 8–12 (afterload reduction as much as recruitment), rapid diuresis (loop ± metolazone), minimal sedation (RASS −1 to −2), early PSV transition.
  • Post-extubation flash-edema risk in marginal-LV patients — plan prophylactic NIV and aggressive pre-extubation diuresis in high-risk (EF < 35%, prior CHF, positive fluid balance).

22Neurologic injury & raised ICP

In acute neurologic injury — TBI, stroke, intracerebral hemorrhage, post-cardiac arrest — the ventilator is a brain protector or a brain killer. Three derangements (hypoxia, hypercapnia, hypocapnia) all worsen outcomes; tight control is the strategy.

22.1 The cerebral autoregulation lesson

Normal cerebral blood flow (CBF) ≈ 50 mL/100 g/min, autoregulated to be constant across MAP 60–150 mmHg. In acute brain injury, autoregulation is impaired or lost — CBF becomes pressure-passive. Two implications:

  1. MAP matters: target cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP = MAP − ICP) of 60–70 mmHg. If ICP monitor in place, vasopressors maintain CPP. Without ICP monitor, target MAP > 80.
  2. PaCO₂ matters: CBF changes ~3% per mmHg PaCO₂ change in the 20–60 mmHg range. Hypercapnia causes vasodilation (↑ CBV → ↑ ICP); hypocapnia causes vasoconstriction (↓ CBV → ↓ ICP, but also ↓ O₂ delivery).

Brief hyperventilation (PaCO₂ 30–35) for impending herniation is a 30-minute bridge while organizing definitive therapy (osmotic, surgical decompression). Sustained hyperventilation worsens outcomes (reduces CBF below ischemic threshold). Once herniation is treated, return PaCO₂ to 35–40 promptly.

The "prophylactic hyperventilation" mistakeOld neurosurgical teaching — "keep them at 30" routinely — is harmful. Don't hyperventilate unless herniating, and only briefly. Same for hyperoxia (PaO₂ > 200): no benefit and probably harmful.

22.4 PEEP and ICP — the controversy

The classic concern: high PEEP transmits to the cranium via raised intrathoracic pressure / impaired jugular drainage. In practice:

  • PEEP up to 10–12 cmH₂O is safe in most patients with intact lung compliance — minimal ICP transmission.
  • PEEP transmission to ICP is greater when lung compliance is high (e.g., normal lung; the pleural pressure rise transmits more directly) and less when lung compliance is low (e.g., ARDS; the lung absorbs the pressure).
  • If ICP rises > 20 with PEEP escalation: lower PEEP, accept lower P/F, treat ICP first.
  • HOB 30° + neutral neck + adequate sedation usually offsets PEEP effect.

22.5 Special situations

  • Post-cardiac arrest: targeted temperature management (TTM) 33–36°C × 24 h. Maintain PaCO₂ 35–45, PaO₂ 80–100 (avoid hyperoxia — TTM trials show worse neuro outcomes with PaO₂ > 300).
  • Status epilepticus: often intubated for airway protection or for sedation (propofol or midazolam infusions). Continuous EEG; deep sedation (RASS −5) until burst suppression in refractory cases.
  • Spinal cord injury (cervical): see §25. High C-spine: lifetime ventilation; consider phrenic pacing.
  • Cushing's response (HTN + bradycardia + irregular respirations): impending herniation. Mannitol or hypertonic saline, hyperventilate briefly, neurosurgery STAT.

22.6 Patient positioning in neuro-ICU

  • Head of bed 30° — improves cerebral venous drainage; reduces ICP by 3–5 mmHg vs supine. Standard for any patient with raised ICP.
  • Neck neutral, no jugular compression — tape ETT loosely; avoid tight cervical collars or restrictive ties. Lateral neck flexion can raise ICP 5–10 mmHg.
  • Avoid Trendelenburg for any reason in raised ICP — even brief head-down positioning during line placement can spike ICP.
  • Reverse Trendelenburg vs HOB 30° — both elevate the head; reverse-T (entire bed tilted) is preferred over HOB-up alone in spinal injury where cervical flexion must be avoided.
Key takeaways
  • In acute brain injury, autoregulation is impaired — CBF becomes pressure-passive; MAP matters (target CPP = MAP − ICP ≥ 60–70 mmHg).
  • PaCO₂ matters: CBF changes ~3% per mmHg; hypercapnia vasodilates (↑ ICP), hypocapnia vasoconstricts (↓ ICP but ↓ O₂ delivery).
  • Default vent: VC-A/C, VT 6–8 mL/kg PBW, RR titrated to PaCO₂ 35–40 (normocapnia), PEEP 5–10, avoid hypoxia, HOB 30°, neutral neck, deep sedation.
  • Brief hyperventilation (PaCO₂ 30–35) is a 30-minute bridge for impending herniation only — sustained hyperventilation worsens outcomes.

23Obesity, pregnancy, post-op generic

A common physiologic theme links these three: chest-wall compliance is reduced, functional residual capacity (FRC) is low, and atelectasis develops within minutes of induction. Standard ARDSnet settings often underventilate and over-restrict these patients — recognize the chest-wall component and adjust.

23.1 Obesity

Obese patients (BMI > 30, especially > 40) have markedly reduced chest-wall compliance from circumferential adiposity, abdominal pressure transmitted to the diaphragm, and supine position. FRC may approach closing volume — basal atelectasis is the rule. Implications:

  • VT by PBW, not actual body weight (this is the most common error). PBW formula: men = 50 + 2.3 × (height in inches − 60); women = 45.5 + 2.3 × (height in inches − 60). VT 6–8 mL/kg PBW.
  • Higher PEEP (10–15, sometimes 18–20) usually needed to splint open the dependent / basal regions. Titrate to oxygenation and Pplat — but recognize that high airway pressure may reflect high pleural pressure, not lung overdistension.
  • Esophageal manometry often reveals that the apparent "high Pplat" is mostly chest-wall pressure. Transpulmonary pressure (Pplat − Pes) is the real driving pressure on the lung. EPVent-2 trial suggested benefit in obese ARDS specifically.15
  • Reverse Trendelenburg or full upright (45–60°) improves FRC and oxygenation; sit obese patients up for procedures and routinely.
  • OHS (obesity hypoventilation syndrome): chronic CO₂ retention in obese patients without lung disease. Often discovered on intubation. Outpatient management with NIV is usually appropriate; do not "correct" PaCO₂ to 40 acutely (alkalosis, weakness, hypokalemia).
Pre-oxygenation in obesityThe desaturation curve is steep — only 1–2 minutes of safe apnea after induction at FRC. Use ramped position (ear at sternal notch level), apneic oxygenation (nasal cannula at 15 L/min during induction), and consider awake intubation in the truly difficult airway.

23.2 Pregnancy

The pregnant respiratory system has its own physiology that the ICU team must respect:

  • Baseline respiratory alkalosis: progesterone-driven. Normal PaCO₂ in pregnancy is 28–32 mmHg with compensatory ↓ HCO₃ ~18–22. Targeting PaCO₂ 40 in pregnancy = severe respiratory acidosis from her perspective — fetal vasoconstriction, placental hypoperfusion. Maintain PaCO₂ 28–32.
  • FRC drops 20% in pregnancy from upward diaphragm displacement; combined with ↑ O₂ consumption (15–20%), apnea desaturation is faster. Pre-oxygenate aggressively and ramp.
  • Left uterine displacement if > 20 weeks gestation — supine position causes aortocaval compression by the gravid uterus, dropping cardiac output 20–30%.
  • Difficult airway: edema, fragile mucosa, breast tissue interfering with laryngoscope. Use a smaller ETT (size 6.5–7.0); have video laryngoscope and supraglottic airway ready.
  • Avoid medications: ACE-I, ARBs, statins, fluoroquinolones, tetracyclines, warfarin (use heparin/LMWH).

23.3 Post-operative respiratory failure

The post-op patient has a specific physiology: residual anesthetic / NMB, narcotic respiratory depression, atelectasis from supine surgery + paralysis, splinting from incision pain, decreased cough.

  • Atelectasis is the dominant problem: most post-op hypoxia is atelectasis, not pneumonia or PE. Treatment: incentive spirometry, deep breathing, early mobilization, adequate analgesia (so the patient can breathe), CPAP in high-risk cases.
  • Residual NMB: TOF ratio < 0.9 at extubation predicts re-intubation. Reverse with sugammadex (rocuronium/vecuronium) 2–4 mg/kg or neostigmine + glycopyrrolate.
  • Opioid-induced hypoventilation: titrate carefully; consider multimodal analgesia (acetaminophen + NSAID + regional + opioid) to minimize opioid dose.
  • Post-op diaphragmatic dysfunction after thoracic, upper abdominal, or cardiac surgery: phrenic nerve injury is the mechanism in cardiac surgery (cold cardioplegia, ICE). May persist weeks-months.
  • Pulmonary edema after major fluid resuscitation: TRALI vs TACO vs cardiogenic vs ARDS. Differentiate by timing (TRALI within 6 h of transfusion), echo, BNP, response to diuretic. (See §27.1.)

23.4 Patient positioning beyond prone — lateral decubitus, semi-Fowler, reverse Trendelenburg

Prone is the headline (§19.5, §32), but other positions have specific indications:

  • Lateral decubitus for unilateral disease: in unilateral pneumonia, unilateral atelectasis, lung contusion, or unilateral pulmonary edema, position the good lung dependent (down). Gravity preferentially perfuses the dependent lung — putting the better lung down means perfusion goes to the better-ventilated side → V/Q matching improves and oxygenation rises within minutes. Reverse this rule for hemoptysis: bad lung down to protect the airway from blood spilling into the good side.
  • Semi-Fowler (HOB 30–45°): standard for any patient with raised ICP, gastric reflux risk, or cardiogenic edema. Reduces VAP rates by ~ 50% vs supine in long-stay vented patients. Avoid in spinal injury without trauma clearance.
  • Reverse Trendelenburg (full bed tilted, head up): preferred over HOB 30° in obese patients (the entire upper body is elevated, including the abdomen) — improves FRC, reduces atelectasis, and may help oxygenation more than HOB-up alone. Useful in pre-oxygenation and during difficult intubation in obesity.
  • Trendelenburg (head down): rarely indicated in vented patients. Once a standard for shock resuscitation, now mostly abandoned — paradoxically reduces venous return in the chest-shifted patient and worsens oxygenation in ARDS. Avoid except for brief central-line placement.
  • Sitting / fully upright: best position for FRC and ventilation in the awake spontaneously-breathing patient. Cooperative awake ARDS patients on HFNC do better sitting up than supine. Combine with awake proning for optimal V/Q in cooperative patients.
The dependent-lung trickFor unilateral lung disease — pneumonia, atelectasis, pulmonary contusion — position the good lung dependent. SpO₂ rises within minutes. Memorize as "good lung down, bad lung up." Exception: hemoptysis (bad lung down to keep blood out of the good airway).
Key takeaways
  • Obese patients: VT by PBW (not actual weight), higher PEEP (10–15, sometimes 18–20), esophageal manometry may show high airway pressure = high pleural pressure (not lung overdistension).
  • Pregnancy: maintain PaCO₂ 28–32 (baseline respiratory alkalosis), not 40; FRC drops 20%, desaturation faster; left uterine displacement > 20 weeks; difficult airway (smaller ETT 6.5–7.0).
  • Post-op: atelectasis is the dominant problem (not pneumonia/PE) — treat with incentive spirometry, CPAP, early mobilization; check TOF for residual NMB; multimodal analgesia.
  • Lateral decubitus: position the good lung dependent for unilateral disease (pneumonia, atelectasis, contusion) — perfusion follows gravity to the better-ventilated side.

24Pulmonary embolism, pulmonary hypertension & RV failure on the vent

The right ventricle is a thin-walled, low-afterload chamber. Anything that raises pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR) — hypoxia, hypercapnia, acidosis, atelectasis, hyperinflation, PEEP, PE, ARDS — pushes it toward failure. The vent strategy is "RV-protective": do no harm to PVR.

24.1 The pulmonary vascular resistance curve

PVR has a U-shaped relationship with lung volume, minimized at FRC. Below FRC, atelectasis kinks extra-alveolar vessels (raised PVR); above FRC, alveolar capillaries are stretched and compressed (raised PVR). Implication for the vent: too little PEEP and too much PEEP both raise RV afterload. The sweet spot is usually 8–12 cmH₂O for most adult lungs.28

Other PVR levers (all bedside-correctable):

  • Hypoxia (PaO₂ < 60) — hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction (HPV) is the dominant acute driver. Goal SpO₂ ≥ 92%.
  • Hypercapnia (PaCO₂ > 50) — direct vasoconstriction. Avoid permissive hypercapnia in RV failure.
  • Acidosis (pH < 7.30) — potentiates hypoxia/CO₂ effect. Buffer or hyperventilate.
  • High mean airway pressure — PEEP, recruitment maneuvers, hyperinflation all transmit to RV outflow.

24.3 The hemodynamic catastrophe — RV-led shock

The RV-failure cascade: ↑ PVR → ↑ RV afterload → RV dilation → septal shift left → impaired LV filling → ↓ cardiac output → coronary hypoperfusion → ↓ RV contractility → ↑ RV dilation → death spiral.

Bedside fix (in order):

  1. Oxygenate (FiO₂ 100% briefly, then titrate); ventilate to PaCO₂ 35–40; buffer pH (sodium bicarbonate to pH > 7.30 acceptable in this acute setting).
  2. Norepinephrine first — supports systemic BP > coronary perfusion to RV.
  3. Inhaled pulmonary vasodilator (iNO 20 ppm) — selective.
  4. Inotrope (dobutamine 2.5–10 µg/kg/min) — RV contractility.
  5. Reduce intravascular volume if RV grossly dilated (cautious diuresis or RRT).
  6. Definitive therapy for cause: thrombolysis (alteplase 100 mg or "half-dose" 50 mg) for confirmed massive PE; surgical embolectomy or catheter-directed therapy at experienced centers.
  7. VA-ECMO if shock refractory to all of the above.
The "fluid bolus that kills"The instinctive response to hypotension is fluid. In RV-led shock, a 500-mL crystalloid bolus can worsen RV dilation, push the septum further left, and precipitate arrest. Echo (or POCUS) before bolus when RV failure is on the differential. If RV looks dilated and akinetic — start pressors and vasodilators, don't volume-load.

24.4 The intubation question in massive PE

Intubating an unstable PE patient often kills them: induction agents drop SVR, positive pressure drops preload, hypoxia from apnea raises PVR. If you must intubate, pre-load with norepinephrine, use ketamine + rocuronium, hand-bag with low rate / low PEEP, and have iNO and ECMO ready. Many massive-PE patients should be on VA-ECMO or in the cath lab for catheter-directed thrombolysis before intubation.

Key takeaways
  • RV is thin-walled, low-afterload chamber; anything raising PVR (hypoxia, hypercapnia, acidosis, atelectasis, hyperinflation, high PEEP) pushes it toward failure.
  • PVR has U-shaped relationship with lung volume, minimized at FRC — both too little and too much PEEP raise RV afterload; sweet spot usually 8–12 cmH₂O.
  • RV-protective vent: VC-A/C, VT 6 mL/kg, RR 14–18 (avoid hypercapnia), PEEP 5–8 (low, avoid high), FiO₂ to SpO₂ > 92%, Pplat ≤ 28, iNO or epoprostenol as bridge.
  • The "fluid bolus that kills": in RV-led shock (echo-confirmed dilated RV), volume worsens RV dilation and septal shift → start pressors and vasodilators, don't volume-load.

25Neuromuscular respiratory failure

In neuromuscular disease (NMD) — Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS), myasthenic crisis, ALS, high cervical cord injury, post-op residual neuromuscular blockade — the lungs are normal but the pump is failing. The diagnostic challenge is recognizing fatigue before arrest; the management challenge is choosing the right pump support.

25.1 The "20–30–40" rule for NIF, FVC, MIP

The classic bedside thresholds for impending respiratory failure (when serial measurements show progression):

  • Forced vital capacity (FVC) < 20 mL/kg (≈ 1 L) — declining secretion clearance, atelectasis risk.
  • Maximum inspiratory pressure (MIP / NIF) < −30 cmH₂O (i.e., the patient cannot generate more negative than −30) — diaphragm fatigue.
  • Maximum expiratory pressure (MEP) < +40 cmH₂O — cough impaired, secretion retention.

Hitting any one of these in a patient with progressive NMD warrants intubation. Don't wait for ABG — by the time PaCO₂ is up, arrest is imminent (the "20–30–40 patient is sicker than the gas").

25.2 Bulbar dysfunction is the other intubation trigger

Aspiration risk from bulbar weakness (tongue, palate, pharyngeal muscles) trumps respiratory mechanics. Signs: nasal speech, dysphagia, drooling, weak cough, weak gag. Intubate early — NIV is contraindicated in bulbar dysfunction (high aspiration rate).

25.4 Disease-specific pearls

  • GBS: peak weakness usually within 2 weeks; expect ~30% need ventilation. Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) or plasma exchange. Tracheostomy if no improvement at 2 wk; many recover within 6 weeks.
  • Myasthenia gravis (MG) crisis: pyridostigmine often paradoxically worsens (cholinergic crisis); plasma exchange or IVIG; steroids cautiously (initial worsening). Avoid succinylcholine and aminoglycosides.
  • ALS: nocturnal NIV is standard of care; intubation is a reversible decision but tracheostomy implies long-term ventilation. Goals-of-care discussion is mandatory before intubation.
  • High cervical cord injury (above C3): permanent diaphragm paralysis, lifetime ventilation. C3–C5 injury: variable diaphragm function. Consider phrenic-nerve pacing in select chronic cases.
  • Post-op residual neuromuscular blockade: TOF ratio < 0.9 at extubation predicts re-intubation; reverse with sugammadex (rocuronium/vecuronium) or neostigmine + glycopyrrolate.

25.5 Tracheostomy timing

For GBS or MG expected to recover within 2–3 weeks: continue with ETT. For prolonged ventilation expected (> 14 days), or for inability to clear secretions, or for patient comfort: tracheostomy at days 7–14 is reasonable. Earlier tracheostomy in NMD does not change mortality but improves comfort, secretion clearance, and may shorten ICU stay.

Key takeaways
  • Neuromuscular disease: lungs are normal, pump is failing — clinical thresholds are FVC < 20 mL/kg, NIF < −30 cmH₂O, MEP < +40 cmH₂O (intubate on these, not ABG).
  • Bulbar dysfunction (nasal speech, dysphagia, drooling) is intubation trigger independent of respiratory mechanics — NIV contraindicated (high aspiration).
  • Vent strategy: VT 8–10 mL/kg PBW (lungs normal, prevent atelectasis without injury), RR to match drive, PEEP 5–8, aggressive secretion clearance, early swallow evaluation.
  • Disease-specific: GBS ~30% need ventilation, peak weakness ~2 wk; MG crisis avoid succinylcholine/aminoglycosides, plasma exchange or IVIG; ALS nocturnal NIV standard, intubation = goals-of-care discussion.

26.3 HFNC — the new standard for hypoxemic failure

Heated humidified nasal cannula at 30–60 L/min flow, FiO₂ 0.21–1.0. Mechanisms: (1) washes out anatomic dead space; (2) generates a small CPAP effect (~3–5 cmH₂O at 60 L/min, mouth closed); (3) heated humidification preserves mucociliary function; (4) tolerable for hours-to-days; (5) allows speech, drinking, oral intake.

Initial settings: 50 L/min flow, FiO₂ titrated to SpO₂ > 92%. Wean flow first (to 30 L/min), then FiO₂.

The ROX index = (SpO₂/FiO₂) / RR. Measured at 2, 6, 12 h. ROX ≥ 4.88 at 12 h predicts HFNC success; ROX < 3.85 at 12 h predicts failure → escalate.31

The HFNC sweet spotHFNC works best for hypoxemic patients without hypercapnia, who can tolerate work of breathing for the time it takes the underlying disease to turn around. It is not a substitute for NIV in hypercapnic COPD or for intubation in worsening hypoxemia. Recheck ROX at scheduled times — don't let "looks comfortable" mask deterioration.

26.4 Contraindications and when to stop the trial

Contraindications to NIV: respiratory or cardiac arrest, inability to protect airway, copious secretions, recent upper GI surgery, hemodynamic instability, severe agitation. Relative: bulbar weakness (high aspiration risk).

Stop the trial and intubate if at 1–2 h: pH falling, RR rising, SpO₂ falling on max FiO₂, encephalopathy worsening, hemodynamic instability, mask intolerance, or HACOR > 5 / ROX < 3.85.

26.5 Helmet NIV — the COVID-era resurgence

A transparent hood that seals at the neck, delivering CPAP or BiPAP without facial pressure. Two reasons to know it: it tolerates higher PEEP (up to 14–15 cmH₂O) and can be worn for hours at a time without the mask-related complications that limit traditional NIV.

HENIVOT (2021) randomized de novo hypoxemic respiratory failure to helmet vs HFNC. Helmet reduced 28-day intubation (30% vs 51%) without mortality difference; benefit was greatest in moderate-severe disease (P/F < 200).33

Asking a non-intubated, hypoxemic patient to lie face-down for hours at a time. Promoted during the COVID pandemic; randomized data are mixed for non-COVID populations.

26.7 Post-extubation NIV/HFNC — prevent the bounce-back

Post-extubation respiratory failure (within 48 h) carries 30–40% mortality and triples ICU length of stay. Two trials reshaped practice:

  • HFNC for hypoxemic respiratory failure without hypercapnia, works best in cooperative patients with normal work of breathing — ROX index ≥ 4.88 at 12 h predicts success.

  • Post-extubation in high-risk patients: HFNC + intermittent BiPAP ≥ 4 h/day × 48 h (Thille 2019) — reduces reintubation and 90-day mortality.
  • Stop trial at 1–2 h if pH falling, RR rising, SpO₂ falling on max FiO₂, mental status worsening, or HACOR > 5 / ROX < 3.85.

27.2 Burn & inhalation injury

Three injuries co-exist and progress on different timelines:

  • Upper-airway thermal injury — from steam, hot smoke. Edema progresses over 24–48 h. Indications for early intubation: facial burns, singed nasal hairs, soot in pharynx, hoarseness, stridor, full-thickness burns > 40% TBSA. Intubate early — once edema sets in, the airway is unmanageable.
  • Lower-airway / parenchymal injury — from soot, particulate, chemical irritants. ARDS-like; develops over 24–72 h.
  • Systemic toxicity — carbon monoxide (CO) and cyanide (CN). 27.3 Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage (DAH)

DAH is the radiographic appearance of bilateral infiltrates with progressive hypoxemia caused by alveolar bleeding rather than edema or pneumonia. Three pathologic patterns:

  • Pulmonary capillaritis — neutrophil-mediated capillary destruction. Causes: ANCA-associated vasculitis (GPA, MPA), anti-GBM (Goodpasture), SLE, drug-induced.
  • Bland hemorrhage — no inflammation. Causes: anticoagulation, mitral stenosis, antiphospholipid syndrome, idiopathic pulmonary hemosiderosis.
  • Diffuse alveolar damage with hemorrhage — superimposed on ARDS.

Recognition: hemoptysis is present in only 1/3 of cases; falling Hb, bilateral infiltrates with hypoxemia, normal-to-elevated DLCO (the only condition where DLCO rises with worsening lung disease — because intra-alveolar Hb takes up CO).

Diagnostic confirmation: bronchoscopy with sequential lavages — progressively bloodier returns confirm DAH. Send for cell count, cytology, microbiology.

Management:

  • Reverse anticoagulation if applicable.
  • High-dose IV methylprednisolone (1 g/day × 3 d) for vasculitis or autoimmune causes.
  • Plasma exchange for anti-GBM disease and severe ANCA disease.
  • Cyclophosphamide or rituximab for ANCA disease.
  • Vent strategy: standard lung-protective; transfuse Hb > 8; correct coagulopathy.
  • VV-ECMO is contraindicated relative to standard ECMO criteria (heparin requirement makes hemorrhage worse) — but can be considered with anticoagulation-free or bivalirudin-based circuits.

27.4 Bronchopleural fistula (BPF)

A persistent communication between an airway and the pleural space — manifested as a continuous air leak through a chest tube despite full lung expansion. Causes: post-pneumonectomy / lobectomy dehiscence, necrotizing pneumonia (Staph aureus, anaerobes, fungi), barotrauma, malignancy.

27.5 End-of-life ventilator withdrawal — terminal extubation

The most consequential decision in many ICUs is when and how to stop. Withdrawing the ventilator is a deliberate clinical procedure, not a failure of care. Done well, it preserves comfort and dignity; done poorly, it causes preventable suffering.

Pre-withdrawal
  • Goals-of-care alignment documented and confirmed with family. Palliative care consult if available.
  • Discontinue non-comfort interventions: vasopressors, antibiotics, nutrition, blood draws, monitors visible to family. Keep what supports comfort.
  • Treat reversible discomfort first: pain, secretions, anxiety. Many patients become more comfortable before withdrawal.
  • Prepare the family: explain the time course (typically minutes-hours), the expected appearance (changes in breathing pattern, terminal secretions, gasping), and that the appearance is not the patient's experience.
  • Position: head of bed elevated; positioned for family access; oral care; eyes closed if possible.

Common pitfalls
  • Under-medicating for fear of "hastening death" — the literature consistently shows that adequately-treated dyspnea does not shorten survival.
  • Premature withdrawal of analgesia after extubation when patient appears settled — agitation often returns within 30–60 min as drugs redistribute.
  • Family unprepared for terminal secretions — counsel ahead that the sound is not distressing to the patient.
  • Overlooking the option of extubation to comfort — many families are relieved to see the tube removed even if the patient is sedated; emotional closure matters.

  • DAH (diffuse alveolar hemorrhage): hemoptysis only 1/3 of cases; falling Hb, bilateral infiltrates, normal-to-elevated DLCO (intra-alveolar Hb takes up CO); bronchoscopy progressive lavages confirm.
  • BPF (bronchopleural fistula): vent preferentially leaks through low-resistance fistula — use PSV (lower leak), minimal VT/RR/PEEP, permit hypercapnia; surgical/bronchoscopic occlusion for refractory leaks.

28.2 Decremental PEEP trial — protocol

  1. Sedate ± paralyze (need passive ventilation).
  2. Set VC mode, VT 6 mL/kg PBW, RR 20–25, I:E 1:1, square flow.
  3. Recruit: PC mode briefly, ΔP 15, PEEP up to 20–25, hold 30–60 s. (Skip recruitment if hemodynamically unstable.)
  4. Switch back to VC. Set PEEP at 20.
  5. Every 4–5 min, lower PEEP by 2; record Cstat = VT / (Pplat − PEEP) at each step.
  6. The PEEP at which Cstat is maximal = "best compliance PEEP." The PEEP just above this (best + 2) is the chosen level — recruits lung with margin.
  7. Brief re-recruitment, then set the chosen PEEP. Monitor oxygenation and hemodynamics over the next hour.
Decremental PEEP in unstable patientsRecruitment maneuvers and high PEEP both drop venous return — can precipitate shock in volume-depleted or vasoplegic patients. Hold pressors / fluid bolus before; stop the maneuver for MAP < 65 or new arrhythmia.

28.3 Best driving-pressure approach (Amato-style) — bedside

Hold VT constant (e.g., 6 mL/kg PBW). Step PEEP from 5 → 25 in 2-cmH₂O increments q5 min. At each step record Pplat and compute ΔP = Pplat − PEEP. Choose the PEEP at which ΔP is minimum. This works in most ICUs without specialty equipment and fits the Amato mediation finding that ΔP is the strongest mortality-correlate.5

28.4 The transpulmonary-pressure approach (EPVent)

Place an esophageal balloon catheter (~ 35–40 cm depth, mid-esophagus). Calibrate to spontaneous swings. Compute:

$$P_{L,\text{end-exp}} \;=\; P_{plat,\text{end-exp-hold}} - P_{es,\text{end-exp}}$$

Target PL,end-exp 0 to +5 cmH₂O — keeps alveoli open at end-expiration. Useful when there's clinical suspicion that high airway pressure reflects high pleural pressure (chest-wall stiffness from obesity, ascites, abdominal hypertension) rather than true alveolar overdistension.

28.4a The stress-index method — bedside PEEP tuning without a balloon

The stress index (SI) reads PEEP optimization off the shape of the airway pressure curve during constant-flow VC ventilation. No esophageal balloon required. The pressure curve fits Paw(t) ≈ a × tSI + b, where SI is the convexity coefficient.

A curve that bends UP (toward the ceiling) at end-inspiration means you've recruited everything and started overstretching — drop PEEP. A curve that flattens (toward the floor) means you're still recruiting on every breath — raise PEEP.

28.5 The hemodynamic price of PEEP — always part of the decision

Each PEEP step has a hemodynamic cost (see Ch 29): ↑ intrathoracic pressure → ↓ venous return + ↑ RV afterload. Track at every step:

  • MAP and pulse pressure variation
  • Lactate trend
  • SvO₂ if available (drops with reduced cardiac output even before MAP changes)
  • Echo stroke-volume response (if PiCCO/Doppler available)

If oxygenation improves but MAP drops or lactate rises: the lung benefit is overwhelmed by the hemodynamic cost. Lower PEEP and accept lower P/F.

The trial that changed PEEP nihilismThe 2017 ART (Alveolar Recruitment for ARDS) trial showed that aggressive recruitment + decremental PEEP titration increased mortality in moderate-severe ARDS — almost certainly from cardiovascular collapse during recruitment.16 Lesson: do not perform high-pressure recruitment maneuvers reflexively; titrate carefully and watch hemodynamics.
Key takeaways
  • Five PEEP titration strategies: (1) FiO₂-based ladder (simple, default), (2) decremental PEEP trial after recruitment (physiologic, ART trial caution), (3) best compliance / lowest ΔP (practical, Amato-based), (4) esophageal pressure-guided (transpulmonary < 25 cmH₂O, for obesity), (5) EIT (real-time imaging, research centers).
  • Hemodynamic cost of PEEP: track MAP, lactate, SvO₂ at every step — if oxygenation improves but hemodynamics worsen, the lung benefit is outweighed.
  • ART trial (2017) showed aggressive recruitment + decremental PEEP increased mortality — likely hemodynamic instability during maneuvers; recruit cautiously, watch closely, abort for MAP < 65.
  • Stress-index visual reading (no calculation needed): linear pressure curve = SI ≈ 1 (good), concave-up = SI > 1 (overdistension, ↓ PEEP), concave-down = SI < 1 (recruitment ongoing, ↑ PEEP).

29Esophageal manometry & transpulmonary pressure

The pleural pressure (P_pl) we cannot measure directly is approximated by esophageal pressure (P_es) measured with an esophageal balloon. The clinically useful quantity is transpulmonary pressure:

$$P_L \;=\; P_{alv} - P_{pl} \;\approx\; P_{aw} - P_{es}$$
  • P_L,end-exp = end-expiratory P_aw (PEEP) − P_es,end-exp. Target 0 to +5 cmH₂O; negative values imply alveolar collapse → raise PEEP.
  • P_L,end-insp = Pplat − P_es,end-insp. Keep < 25 cmH₂O; higher implies overdistension → lower VT or PEEP.

Highest yield in obesity, pregnancy, abdominal hypertension, severe chest-wall pathology, where airway pressures alone overestimate lung stress.

29.3 Lung stress & strain — the Gattinoni framework

ΔP and mechanical power are surrogates. The actual mechanical injury currency is lung stress (the pressure across the alveolar wall) and strain (the relative deformation of the lung from its resting size).34

$$\text{Stress} \;=\; P_L \;=\; P_{alv} - P_{pl} \;\approx\; P_{aw} - P_{es}$$
$$\text{Strain} \;=\; \frac{\Delta V}{\text{FRC}} \;=\; \frac{V_T + V_{\text{recruited}}}{\text{FRC}}$$
  • Stress threshold for injury: PL > 25 cmH₂O. Above this, alveolar wall stretch exceeds the breaking strength of the extracellular matrix.
  • Strain threshold for injury: total strain > 1.5–2.0 (i.e., the lung inflates to 1.5–2× its FRC volume during a tidal breath). Strain > 2 reliably produces VILI in animal models.
  • The relationship: stress and strain are linked by lung-specific elastance (EL,specific ≈ 13.5 cmH₂O in humans). Stress = EL,specific × strain. So strain of 2 corresponds to stress of ~ 27 — right at the injury threshold.

Why this beats ΔP alone: ΔP is the airway-pressure surrogate of stress, but it doesn't account for chest-wall stiffness. An obese ARDS patient with airway ΔP 18 may have lung ΔP (transpulmonary) of only 10 — the chest wall is doing most of the resisting. ΔP-driven settings would underventilate this patient. Esophageal manometry measures the lung-only fraction directly.

29.4 Cardiopulmonary interactions of mechanical ventilation

Positive-pressure ventilation does not just oxygenate — it changes preload, afterload, and the transmural pressures the heart sees. Understanding this is what separates the pulmonologist from the critical-care physician at the bedside.

Practical implications
  • Post-intubation hypotension — almost always caused by sedation + loss of sympathetic tone + ↓ preload from positive pressure. Anticipate; have IVF ready, push-dose pressors at the bedside.
  • Cardiogenic pulmonary edema — NIV/positive pressure works partly by oxygenating, but mostly by unloading the LV (↓ transmural pressure = ↓ afterload).
  • RV failure / pulmonary hypertension — high PEEP, high VT, hypoxia, hypercapnia, and acidosis all raise pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR). Aim for low VT, normoxia, gentle ventilation; consider inhaled pulmonary vasodilators (nitric oxide [NO], iloprost, epoprostenol) as bridge.
  • PEEP titration in shock — every PEEP step has a hemodynamic price. Watch MAP and lactate; titrate slowly.
  • Reverse Trendelenburg in obese / abdominal-hypertension patients improves both compliance and hemodynamics.
  • Auto-PEEP obstructs venous return; abrupt hypotension on the vent in COPD/asthma often = auto-PEEP. Disconnect the circuit and let the chest empty.
The single equation worth rememberingLV transmural pressure = LV cavity pressure − pleural pressure. When pleural pressure rises (positive-pressure inspiration, or tightening Pplat), LV transmural pressure falls — the heart "feels" less afterload. Same in reverse: a large negative inspiratory effort raises transmural pressure (afterload) and can precipitate flash edema in the failing LV.

29.5 The Guyton view — venous return × Frank-Starling, and how the ventilator moves both curves

Arthur Guyton's insight (1955) was that the cardiac output a patient generates is the intersection of two curves that share the same axes: the venous-return curve (cardiac output as a function of right atrial pressure, PRA) and the Frank-Starling curve (cardiac output as a function of preload). Mechanical ventilation moves both curves — and seeing this on one figure clarifies almost every hemodynamic question in critical care.25

Figure 9b. Guyton's two-curve model. Cardiac output sits where the venous-return curve and the Frank-Starling curve cross. Mechanical ventilation moves both curves: ↑ intrathoracic pressure compresses the great veins → drops the plateau of the venous-return curve (less venous return at any PRA); high lung volume / hyperinflation also raises pulmonary vascular resistance and shifts the Frank-Starling curve right (the RV needs more preload to pump the same output). The new operating point sits at lower CO. After Kenny's integration of the Guyton and Campbell frameworks.25
  • Mean systemic filling pressure (MSFP) — the x-intercept of the venous-return curve. This is the upstream "head" pressure driving blood back to the right heart, set by total blood volume and venous tone. Volume-loading or vasopressors raise MSFP and shift the venous-return curve up; vasodilation, sedation, and bleeding drop it.
  • Resistance to venous return (RVR) — the slope. ↑ RVR (high PEEP, hyperinflation, abdominal compartment syndrome) lowers cardiac output at every PRA.
  • Frank-Starling reserve — a patient on the steep portion is preload-responsive (will benefit from a fluid bolus); a patient on the flat plateau is not (and may decompensate).
  • The mechanical ventilator's hemodynamic move — every PEEP increase (a) drops the venous-return plateau via raised intrathoracic pressure and (b) may shift Frank-Starling right via increased RV afterload. Track MAP, lactate, and (if available) Doppler/echo stroke-volume response with each step.
Reading the diagram clinicallyIf the patient is hypotensive after PEEP up-titration: ask whether the venous-return curve dropped (volume / pressors), whether the Frank-Starling curve shifted right (RV strain — echo, troponin), or both. The intervention follows the diagnosis: more volume / vasopressor, lower PEEP, recruit only what helps oxygenation, treat the RV.
Key takeaways
  • Transpulmonary pressure (P_L) = P_alveolar − P_pleural ≈ P_airway − P_esophageal; target P_L,end-exp 0 to +5 cmH₂O (alveoli open), P_L,end-insp < 25 cmH₂O (not overdistended).
  • Lung stress (P_L) and strain (ΔV/FRC) are the injury currency; stress > 25 cmH₂O and strain > 1.5–2.0 cross thresholds for VILI — esophageal manometry reveals when high airway pressure = high pleural pressure (chest wall), not lung injury.
  • Cardiopulmonary interactions: ↑ intrathoracic pressure ↓ venous return + ↑ RV afterload; ↑ pleural pressure ↓ LV transmural pressure (afterload); every PEEP step has hemodynamic cost.
  • Guyton curves: ↑ intrathoracic pressure drops venous-return plateau AND may shift Frank-Starling right via RV strain — track both; low-output state post-PEEP = volume, pressors, or lower PEEP.

30Driving pressure & mechanical power

Two single numbers concentrate most of what's known about ventilator-induced lung injury into bedside-actionable thresholds: driving pressure (ΔP) and mechanical power (MP). Both are computed from quantities already on the screen. Both are independent mortality predictors. Both should be checked at every rounds.

30.1 Driving pressure (ΔP)

$$\Delta P \;=\; P_{plat} - \text{PEEP} \;=\; \frac{V_T}{C_{stat}}$$

Conceptually: ΔP is the cyclic stress applied to the lung above PEEP each breath. It normalizes tidal volume to the lung that's actually open (the "baby lung" in ARDS) — which is why ΔP captures injury better than VT alone.

Amato's 2015 mediation analysis of > 3500 ARDSnet patients showed that ΔP was the variable most strongly correlated with mortality — and that the protective effect of low VT and high PEEP was mediated almost entirely through their effect on ΔP.5 Hazard ratio: each additional 7 cmH₂O of ΔP increased mortality by ~40%.

Targets: ΔP < 15 cmH₂O for ARDS; ideally < 13. If ΔP is high despite VT 6 mL/kg PBW, the lung is too stiff and you need to find a way to either increase compliance (PEEP titration to recruit more lung) or reduce VT further (4 mL/kg if needed). Mortality climbs sharply above 18.

The "compliance-normalized" insightTwo ARDS patients with identical 6 mL/kg PBW VT can have wildly different ΔP — one with Cstat 30 (ΔP = 13) doing fine, another with Cstat 15 (ΔP = 26) on the edge. The vent settings look identical; only the ΔP reveals the difference.

30.2 Mechanical power (MP)

ΔP captures stress per breath; mechanical power captures stress integrated over time — energy delivered to the lung per minute. It accounts for the four levers that all matter: VT, ΔP, RR, and peak flow.

Gattinoni's simplified formula:

$$\text{MP (J/min)} \;=\; 0.098 \times \text{RR} \times V_T \times \left(P_{IP} - \tfrac{1}{2}\Delta P\right)$$

Where: 0.098 is the conversion constant (mL × cmH₂O → J); RR is respiratory rate (breaths/min); VT is tidal volume in liters; PIP is peak inspiratory pressure; ΔP = Pplat − PEEP.

Worked example: ARDS patient on VC: VT 0.4 L, RR 24, PIP 35, Pplat 28, PEEP 12. ΔP = 28 − 12 = 16. MP = 0.098 × 24 × 0.4 × (35 − 8) = 0.098 × 24 × 0.4 × 27 = 25 J/min. This is well above the threshold associated with mortality. To lower it: drop RR (every −1 RR = ~ −1 J/min), drop VT, drop ΔP via PEEP optimization, drop peak flow.

Threshold: MP > 17 J/min has been associated with increased mortality in observational data.17 Some experts use > 12 J/min as a more conservative target.

30.3 The four levers to reduce mechanical power (in order of impact)

  1. Respiratory rate — biggest leverage; MP is linear with RR. Drop from 28 → 22 = ~20% MP reduction.
  2. Tidal volume — linear with VT. 6 → 5 mL/kg = ~17% MP reduction.
  3. Driving pressure — directly proportional. Address via PEEP recruitment or VT reduction.
  4. Peak inspiratory flow / PIP — affects the resistive component. Switch to decelerating flow waveform; reduce peak flow if hemodynamics allow.
The single bedside checkIf you don't know the patient's ΔP and MP, you don't know how protective the vent is. Both should be on every flowsheet for vented ICU patients, alongside Pplat, RR, FiO₂, and PEEP. Track trends — a slowly rising ΔP over a shift is the warning sign of fibroproliferative ARDS or worsening compliance.

30.4 The "lung-protective hierarchy" — what to optimize when

When all targets cannot be met simultaneously, prioritize:

  1. Pplat ≤ 30 (some say 28) — non-negotiable barotrauma threshold
  2. ΔP ≤ 15 — strongest mortality association
  3. VT 4–6 mL/kg PBW — ARMA foundation
  4. MP < 17 J/min — emerging energy-based metric
  5. Permissive hypercapnia (pH ≥ 7.20) acceptable to achieve the above
Key takeaways
  • Driving pressure (ΔP = Pplat − PEEP): the cyclic stress applied to the open lung; each additional 7 cmH₂O correlates with ~40% ↑ mortality (Amato 2015); target < 15, ideally < 13.
  • Mechanical power (MP = 0.098 × RR × VT × [PIP − ½ΔP] in J/min): integrates stress over time; > 17 J/min associated with mortality; reduces via ↓RR (most impactful), ↓VT, ↓ΔP, ↓peak flow.
  • Compliance-normalized view: two patients with identical 6 mL/kg VT can have ΔP 13 vs 26 based on Cstat alone — only ΔP reveals which patient is protected and which is on the edge.
  • Lung-protective hierarchy: Pplat ≤ 30 (non-negotiable), ΔP ≤ 15 (strongest mortality association), VT 4–6 mL/kg (ARMA foundation), MP < 17 J/min (emerging), permit pH ≥ 7.20 to achieve the above.

A recruitment maneuver (RM) is a brief, controlled application of high airway pressure intended to open collapsed alveoli. Three techniques are described, and the post-ART era has narrowed their indications considerably.

Reasonable indications (use cautiously, after ART trial):

  • Refractory hypoxemia in moderate-severe ARDS where prone is unavailable or contraindicated

  • Pre-PEEP-titration to define the upper limit (decremental titration)

Contraindications / abort criteria:

  • Hemodynamic instability: SBP < 90 or norepinephrine > 0.3 mcg/kg/min
  • Pneumothorax or bronchopleural fistula (will worsen leak)
  • Severe RV dysfunction
  • Raised ICP
  • Significant air leak at baseline
  • Abort for: MAP drop > 20 mmHg, SpO₂ < 88%, new arrhythmia, hemoptysis, subcutaneous emphysema

31.3 The ART trial caution

The Alveolar Recruitment for ARDS Trial (ART, 2017) randomized > 1000 patients with moderate-severe ARDS to aggressive recruitment + decremental PEEP vs ARDSnet low-PEEP. Mortality was higher in the recruitment group (55% vs 49%, p = 0.041) — driven primarily by hemodynamic instability during the recruitment maneuvers.16 Lesson: recruitment is not free; the hemodynamic price can outweigh the oxygenation gain. Reserve for select patients, monitor closely, and abort early.

Key takeaways
  • Recruitment maneuvers: sustained inflation (CPAP 30–40 × 30 s, fast but hemodynamically risky), stepwise PC ramp, or cycling sighs (physiologic, less disturbance).
  • Reasonable indications (post-ART caution): refractory hypoxemia when prone unavailable, post-suctioning atelectasis, pre-PEEP-titration definition of upper limit.
  • Contraindications: SBP < 90, on high-dose norepi (> 0.3 µg/kg/min), PTX/BPF, severe RV dysfunction, raised ICP, significant air leak — abort for MAP drop > 20, SpO₂ < 88%, arrhythmia, hemoptysis.
  • ART trial showed aggressive recruitment + decremental PEEP increased mortality from hemodynamic instability — the lung benefit was outweighed.

32Prone positioning

Prone positioning is the single intervention with the largest mortality benefit in moderate-severe ARDS — larger than NMB, larger than any pharmacologic adjunct, and complementary to lung-protective ventilation. Yet it remains underused — at least 30% of eligible patients never get proned.

32.1 The physiologic mechanisms

  • Improved V/Q matching: in supine ARDS, ventilation is preferentially distributed to ventral (anterior) lung, but perfusion goes to dorsal (gravity-dependent) lung — V/Q mismatch. Prone reverses this distribution: more uniform ventilation, perfusion still goes to dependent (now anterior) regions, V/Q improves.
  • Recruitment of dorsal lung: dorsal regions in supine ARDS are flooded/atelectatic. Prone removes the gravitational compression and recruits these regions.
  • Mechanical homogenization: distributes stress more evenly across the lung, reducing the focal overdistension at the boundary between aerated and atelectatic regions.
  • Improved RV function: dorsal lung recruitment lowers PVR, reducing RV afterload (often the proximate cause of death in severe ARDS).
  • Reduced ventilator-induced lung injury: more homogeneous strain reduces VILI.

32.2 PROSEVA — the trial that defined modern practice

PROSEVA randomized 466 patients with severe ARDS (P/F < 150, FiO₂ ≥ 0.6, PEEP ≥ 5) to prone ≥ 16 h/day × ≥ 4 days vs supine. 28-day mortality: 16% prone vs 33% supine (HR 0.39, p < 0.001) — among the largest treatment effects ever demonstrated for an ICU intervention.10

32.3 Pre-prone checklist

  1. Secure airway: confirm ETT depth on CXR; tape securely.
  2. Secure lines: central line, arterial line, NG/OG. Hold tube feeds 1 h prior; aspirate stomach.
  3. Eye care: lubricant + tape closed; no contact lenses.
  4. Padding: forehead, chin, shoulders, elbows, hips, knees, dorsal feet. Female patients: protect breasts; male: protect genitals.
  5. Sedation deep (RASS −5) and consider NMB if dyssynchrony.
  6. 5-person team minimum: head/airway, two lateral, one logistics, one documentation/monitoring.
  7. Pre-suction the airway. Pre-oxygenate to SpO₂ ≥ 95% if possible.
  8. Plan the supinate-emergency: which person leads if arrest or extubation in prone?

32.4 The maneuver

The "sheet roll" technique: place rolled sheet on each side of the patient. Coordinate count: lift, slide laterally, then 180° rotation. Reposition arms in "swimmer's pose" (one up, one down, alternated q2h). HOB elevated 10–25° (reverse Trendelenburg). Reposition head q2h to prevent pressure injury.

32.5 Vent adjustments after proning

  • First 30 min: SpO₂ may transiently fall before improving — don't panic.
  • 1–2 h: Pplat usually decreases (improved compliance from recruited lung) — opportunity to drop PEEP 2–4 cmH₂O if oxygenation has improved.
  • Re-check Pplat, ΔP, P/F, hemodynamics at 30 min, 1 h, and 4 h after each turn.
  • Maintain ≥ 16 h/day in prone; supinate AM for procedures, lines, CT imaging.

32.6 Stopping criteria

  • Success: P/F > 150 supine on PEEP ≤ 10 and FiO₂ ≤ 0.6, sustained ≥ 4 h.
  • Non-response: no improvement after 4 sessions (typically 4 days).
  • Life-threatening complication: unplanned extubation, cardiac arrest, vascular access loss, severe hemodynamic instability that does not recover.

32.7 Awake proning

Proning of awake, non-intubated, hypoxemic patients gained widespread use in the COVID era. Modest evidence that it delays/avoids intubation in cooperative patients with isolated hypoxemic respiratory failure. Practical: have patients prone for 1–2 h cycles ≥ 8 h/day; effective in ~50% who can tolerate it. Less effective if work of breathing is high.

32.8 Common complications

  • Pressure injury (face, chest, knees) — mitigated by padding and head repositioning
  • Brachial plexus injury — careful arm positioning ("swimmer's pose")
  • Endotracheal tube migration / unplanned extubation — re-secure, check depth pre/post
  • Vascular line dislodgement — secure at multiple points
  • Facial edema — usually transient; subsides hours after returning supine
  • Conjunctival injury / corneal abrasions — taped closure + lubricant
The "16-hour failure" — a misnomerIf a patient fails to improve in the first prone session, don't abandon — the V/Q matching benefit may take 24–72 h to manifest. PROSEVA used 17 ± 3 h × 4–7 days; many responders are slow.
Key takeaways
  • Prone positioning in moderate-severe ARDS (P/F < 150): PROSEVA showed 28-day mortality 16% prone vs 33% supine (HR 0.39, p < 0.001) — one of the largest treatment effects ever demonstrated.
  • Mechanisms: improved V/Q matching (ventilation anterior, perfusion now to anterior too), recruitment of dorsal lung, mechanical homogenization (less focal overdistension), improved RV function (↓ PVR).
  • Pre-proning: secure ETT & lines, eye protection, padding (forehead, chin, shoulders, elbows, hips, knees, dorsal feet), deep sedation (RASS −5), 5-person team, pre-suction.
  • Stopping: P/F > 150 supine on PEEP ≤ 10 & FiO₂ ≤ 0.6 (≥ 4 h), or no response after 4 sessions, or life-threatening complication; awake proning in cooperative patients delays/avoids intubation ~50%.

33NMB and ECMO

Two adjunct therapies that share the same indication — refractory ARDS — and the same risk-benefit equation: meaningful gain when used at the right severity and timing, harm when overused or delayed.

33.1 Neuromuscular blockade in ARDS

Mechanism of benefit: eliminates patient-vent dyssynchrony (especially double-triggering and reverse triggering); ensures lung-protective tidal volumes; reduces oxygen consumption (paralysis ↓ VO₂ by 10–15%); may reduce P-SILI.

The trial trajectory:

  • ACURASYS (2010): 340 severe ARDS patients (P/F < 150). Cisatracurium 15-mg bolus + 37.5 mg/h × 48 h vs sedation alone. 90-day mortality 32% vs 41% (HR 0.68, p = 0.04). Established early NMB in severe ARDS.18
  • ROSE (2019): 1006 moderate-severe ARDS patients. Continuous NMB × 48 h with deep sedation vs lighter sedation without routine NMB. No mortality benefit. Stopped early for futility. Showed cardiac events more common in the NMB group.11

Current synthesis: NMB is not routine. Reserve for:

  • Refractory dyssynchrony despite optimized sedation
  • Persistent driving pressure > 15 despite VT/PEEP optimization
  • Severe ARDS while establishing prone/ECMO
  • Ventilator-induced lung injury concerns from large patient effort

33.2 NMB practical use (see also Ch 34)

  • Cisatracurium first-line: 15-mg bolus + 37.5 mg/h, max 48–72 h. Hofmann elimination (organ-independent).
  • Daily TOF at orbicularis oculi or adductor pollicis; target 1–2/4 twitches.
  • Always with deep sedation (RASS −5; verify no awareness), eye care, DVT prophylaxis, daily reassessment to discontinue.
  • Avoid concurrent steroids if possible — risk of ICU-acquired weakness rises sharply.

33.3 VV-ECMO — when to refer

EOLIA criteria (2018):13

  • P/F < 50 mmHg for > 3 h, OR
  • P/F < 80 mmHg for > 6 h, OR
  • pH < 7.25 with PaCO₂ ≥ 60 mmHg for > 6 h
  • Despite optimized vent (RR 35, Pplat ≤ 32, PEEP ≥ 10, NMB, prone trial)
  • Reversible cause; < 7 days of high-pressure ventilation; no major contraindications

Trial result: EOLIA was statistically neutral (frequentist, p = 0.09) but stopped early for crossover. Bayesian re-analysis (2018) suggested 96% posterior probability of mortality benefit. Current consensus: refer early when criteria approached.

33.4 ECMO physiology and the "ultra-protective vent"

33.5 ECMO contraindications

  • Absolute: irreversible underlying lung disease without transplant candidacy, severe brain injury (anoxic, hemorrhagic), advanced metastatic malignancy, multi-organ failure with poor prognosis
  • Relative: age > 70, BMI > 50 (institution-dependent), high-pressure ventilation > 7 days (worse outcomes), uncontrolled bleeding, immunocompromise
  • Always discuss with the family pre-cannulation — goals of care, expected duration, complication risks, decannulation timing

33.6 Major complications of ECMO

  • Bleeding (~30%): GI, intracranial, surgical sites. Anticoagulation balance is delicate.
  • Thrombosis: circuit clotting, line thrombosis, stroke (5–10%).
  • Cannulation injury: vascular damage, malposition.
  • Hemolysis: from circuit; monitor LDH, free Hb.
  • Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia: switch to bivalirudin if HIT confirmed.
  • Differential hypoxia ("North-South syndrome") — primarily VA-ECMO; the VV-ECMO version is less common.
  • Right-heart strain from cannula impingement on the IVC/RA.
Key takeaways
  • NMB in ARDS: ACURASYS (2010) showed benefit (cisatracurium 15 mg bolus + 37.5 mg/h × 48 h, 90-day mortality 32% vs 41%); ROSE (2019) showed no routine benefit, stopped for futility.
  • Current use: NMB is NOT routine — reserve for refractory dyssynchrony, persistent ΔP > 15, severe ARDS on prone/ECMO, P-SILI concerns; always with deep sedation (RASS −5), TOF monitoring, eye care, DVT prophylaxis.
  • VV-ECMO criteria: P/F < 50 for > 3 h, OR < 80 for > 6 h, OR pH < 7.25 with PaCO₂ ≥ 60 for > 6 h, despite optimized vent; reversible cause, < 7 days of high-pressure vent.
  • Settings on ECMO: ultra-protective vent (VT 3–4 mL/kg PBW, RR 4–10, PEEP 10–15, FiO₂ 0.30–0.50) — lung rests while oxygenation/CO₂ removal are via circuit.

34Sedation, analgesia & neuromuscular blockade — drug reference

The wrong sedation strategy doubles ventilator days, triples delirium, and worsens long-term cognitive outcomes. The right one — analgesia first, lightest sedation that achieves the goal, daily interruption — is one of the highest-leverage things you can do for a vented patient. Drug doses and titration are second to which patient gets what.

34.1 The ICU sedation philosophy — "analgosedation" + lightest possible

  • Analgesia first: most ICU agitation is pain. Treat pain (fentanyl bolus / drip) before reaching for sedation.
  • Target light sedation (RASS 0 to −2) unless specific indication for deeper. The ABC trial (Awakening + Breathing Coordination) and the SPICE-III trial confirmed: lighter is better for outcomes.21
  • Daily sedation interruption (SAT): pause sedation each morning. If the patient wakes up calmly → SBT. If agitated → restart at 50% prior dose.
  • Avoid benzodiazepines as routine sedation — strong association with delirium and prolonged ventilation. Use propofol or dexmedetomidine first-line.
  • Treat delirium nonpharmacologically first: orient, mobilize, reduce noise, restore sleep-wake cycle. Antipsychotics have weak evidence; quetiapine 25–100 mg at night is reasonable for hyperactive delirium.

Part VI

Liberation

Most patients leave the vent more by way of careful liberation than by elegant initial settings. Daily readiness, structured SBT, and disciplined extubation criteria save ICU days.

35Spontaneous breathing trials

Daily SBT readiness criteria
  • Underlying problem improving.
  • SpO₂ ≥ 90% on FiO₂ ≤ 0.4 and PEEP ≤ 5–8.
  • Hemodynamic stability without escalating vasopressors.
  • Adequate cough, manageable secretions.
  • Awake and follows commands (or progressing toward it; reasonable RASS −1 to 0).

36Extubation & tracheostomy decisions

36.1 The four extubation gates

  1. Mental status — follows commands; will protect airway.
  2. Cough — adequate, brisk, productive on demand. Voluntary "white card" cough strength predicts success.
  3. Secretions — manageable burden, infrequent suctioning.
  4. Cuff leak — present (≥ 110 mL air loss with cuff down for VC, or audible leak); no leak suggests airway edema and risk of post-extubation stridor — consider IV steroid (methylprednisolone 20 mg q4h × 4 doses prior, or 40 mg single dose 4 h prior).

36.2 Post-extubation respiratory support

  • High-risk (heart failure, COPD, age > 65, multiple co-morbidities, prolonged intubation): high-flow nasal cannula (HFNC) + intermittent NIV reduces re-intubation vs HFNC alone.20
  • Average risk: HFNC alone is non-inferior to facemask; preferred for comfort.

  2. Cuff requires more air than usual to seal: cuff valve damage, pilot balloon damage, or true rupture. Plan for ETT exchange.
  3. Sudden complete loss of cuff seal: tube migration up to vocal cords, cuff rupture, or accidental extubation. Evaluate immediately.
  4. VT delivered > VT exhaled: leak around cuff, in circuit, or in the lung (BPF, see §27.4).
  5. Persistent low cuff pressure despite re-inflation: pilot tube valve failure — temporize with hemostat clamp on pilot tube; plan ETT exchange.
Cuff complications
  • Tracheal mucosal injury / pressure necrosis — cuff pressure > 30 cmH₂O for hours; long-term cuff > 25. Maintain 20–30; rotate the cuff position with each ETT-tape change.
  • Tracheomalacia — late effect of prolonged cuff injury; cartilage softening leads to dynamic collapse on extubation. Requires tracheal stenting in severe cases.
  • Tracheo-esophageal fistula — the catastrophic late complication, usually with concurrent NG/OG tube creating posterior pressure. Suspect if new copious secretions or feeds in the ETT.
  • Tracheo-innominate fistula — also a late complication, more common with tracheostomy than ETT. Sentinel bleed → may be followed by exsanguinating hemorrhage. Overinflate cuff to tamponade and emergent OR.
The cuff-check on every roundsMake cuff pressure a routine vital sign — once per shift at minimum. The 30 seconds it takes to check prevents weeks of late tracheal complications.

36.3 Tracheostomy — indications, timing, technique

Indications:

  • Anticipated need for ventilation > 14 days
  • Failure to wean despite optimization
  • Secretion management burden (can't clear with suctioning)
  • Neuromuscular weakness (high cervical SCI, ALS, persistent diaphragmatic dysfunction)
  • Upper-airway obstruction / planned head-and-neck surgery (often emergent)
  • Comfort and reduced sedation needs in long-term ventilation

Timing: the TracMan trial (Tracheostomy Management in critical care) and meta-analyses show no mortality difference between early (≤ 4 days) and late (≥ 10 days) tracheostomy in critically ill adults. Early trach does reduce sedation needs and ventilator days but at the cost of operating on patients who would have been extubated. Practical default: re-evaluate at day 10–14; tracheostomy if vent dependence likely > 7 more days.

36.6 Tracheostomy weaning & decannulation

Sequential weaning:

  1. Wean ventilator settings as in standard SBT (PSV → trach mask).
  2. Trach collar (T-piece) trials: increasing periods of unassisted breathing.
  3. Cuff deflation: tolerate cuff down without aspiration. Pre-test with methylene blue swallow if concerned.
  4. Downsizing: smaller tube (e.g., 8 → 6) to facilitate airflow around the tube; precedes capping.
  5. Capping trial: occlude the tube while patient breathes around it through the upper airway. Confirms patent upper airway. Begin with brief periods, progress to 24–48 h sustained.
  6. Decannulation: after 24–48 h of cap tolerance with adequate cough and secretion clearance. Cover stoma with occlusive dressing; closes within 5–7 days.

36.7 Passy-Muir valve (speaking valve)

One-way valve that opens on inspiration (gas in via the trach), closes on expiration (gas out via the upper airway through the vocal cords) — restores phonation, swallow coordination, smell, and dignity. Requires:

  • Cuff fully deflated (no air leak around cuff to upper airway = no phonation)
  • Patient tolerates cuff down without aspiration
  • Adequate airflow around the tube (downsize if needed)
  • Awake / cooperative

Place the valve only after confirming cuff is fully deflated (a partially-inflated cuff with valve in place creates a closed system → catastrophic barotrauma in seconds). Have a "cuff-deflate-first" checklist on the trach algorithm.

36.8 Swallow evaluation in the trach patient

Aspiration risk persists after trach in ~50%. Bedside evaluation by speech-language pathology should occur before any oral intake. Modified barium swallow or fiberoptic endoscopic evaluation of swallowing (FEES) confirms safe swallow. Cuff status during swallow: fully deflated (allowing normal upper-airway flow); inflated cuff increases dysphagia by tethering the larynx.

36.9 Major tracheostomy complications

  • Early bleeding (< 48 h): venous oozing usually self-limited; arterial bleeding (innominate artery — late, 1–6 weeks) is catastrophic — manage by overinflating the cuff to tamponade and emergent OR transport.
  • Tracheo-esophageal fistula: late complication from cuff over-inflation or tube erosion. Suspect with new copious secretions or feeds appearing in the trach.
  • Tracheal stenosis (10–15% at 6 months): from cuff pressure or excessive granulation. Maintain cuff pressure 20–30 cmH₂O.
  • Stomal infection: typical wound care; rarely severe.
  • Accidental decannulation: especially < 7 days post-trach (immature tract, high re-cannulation risk). Have replacement trach + smaller spare at bedside; if cannot recannulate, oral intubation through the cords.
  • Tube blockage: mucous plug — first-line is removing the inner cannula and cleaning. If blocked at the level of the outer cannula: emergency tube change.
The "lost airway" emergency in a tracheostomy patientIf the patient is decompensating and the cause is unclear, follow the green-pathway algorithm (UK National Tracheostomy Safety Project): (1) call for help; (2) apply oxygen to face AND stoma; (3) check tube patency (suction, then remove inner cannula); (4) if still obstructed, deflate cuff (allows airflow around); (5) if still obstructed, remove the tracheostomy tube; (6) ventilate via face if upper airway intact, or via stoma. Mature tract (> 7 days) means stomal access is reliable; immature tract may close → require oral intubation.

36.10 Bronchoscopy through the ETT — settings, procedure, complications

Fiberoptic bronchoscopy (FOB) at the bedside is one of the most common procedures done on the ventilated patient. The trade-off: the scope occludes a substantial fraction of the ETT lumen, so resistance and airway pressures spike during scope passage. A few preparatory moves prevent most of the trouble.

Indications

  • BAL for VAP / PJP / unusual organisms in immunocompromised patients
  • Mucus-plug clearance / lobar atelectasis not resolving with suctioning
  • Post-extubation stridor or respiratory distress — visualize larynx and subglottis
  • Hemoptysis localization
  • Foreign-body retrieval
  • Suspected tracheal stenosis or late ETT/trach complications

Pre-procedure preparation

  1. Increase FiO₂ to 1.0 for 5 minutes — the scope displaces oxygen and desat happens fast.
  2. Sedation: target RASS −3 to −4. Bolus propofol 0.5–1 mg/kg or midazolam 1–2 mg IV right before.
  3. Brief NMB (rocuronium 0.6–1 mg/kg or succinylcholine 1–1.5 mg/kg) prevents coughing/bucking and reduces hypoxemia — strongly consider in difficult cases.
  4. Deep tracheal suction first — clear the secretions you would otherwise blindly push around.
  5. Document baseline HR, BP, SpO₂; have epinephrine and bronchodilator at bedside.

Equipment & sizing — what fits in what

Standard adult fiberscope OD is 5.7 mm. Match scope to ETT to avoid catastrophic occlusion:

Ventilator settings during the scope

By Poiseuille, halving lumen radius increases resistance ~16×. A 5.7 mm scope in a 7.5 ETT raises Raw 5-fold; in a 7.0 ETT, 10–15-fold. Maintain PEEP. Reduce alarm sensitivities for the duration only.

BAL technique (typical for VAP)

  1. Advance scope through swivel adapter to main carina.
  2. Inspect anatomy: secretions, edema, blood, masses.
  3. Wedge in the targeted segmental bronchus (right lower lobe most common for VAP).
  4. Inject 50 mL warm sterile saline; immediately suction back into a sterile trap. Repeat × 3–4 (total lavage 150–200 mL). Discard the first aliquot if testing for VAP (proximal contamination).
  5. Withdraw; document any bleeding or unusual findings.
  6. Send: Gram stain, quantitative culture (≥ 10⁴ CFU/mL = positive), viral PCR; add fungal culture / silver stain / DFA / mycobacterial stains if immunocompromised.

About 10–15% of patients who pass an SBT and are extubated will need to be re-intubated within 48–72 hours. Extubation failure is independently associated with prolonged ICU stay and mortality. The causes are classically split into airway / mechanical and cardiopulmonary, but the latter is consistently underestimated.25

Practical bedside framework
  • SBT-induced changes that predict cardiogenic failure: rising B-line count on lung US, rising NT-proBNP > 20% from baseline, ↑ hemoconcentration (Hb ↑ > 5%, surrogate for fluid shift into the lung), new mitral E/e′ > 13 on echo. Treat with diuresis and re-attempt; consider NIV bridge.

The Kenny insightThe transition from positive-pressure to spontaneous ventilation reverses every cardiopulmonary interaction described in §29.4 within seconds. Key takeaways
  • Four extubation gates: (1) mental status (follows commands, airway protection), (2) cough (adequate, brisk, productive), (3) secretions (manageable, suctioning < q2h), (4) cuff leak (≥ 110 mL or audible = no leak → consider IV methylprednisolone 4 h prior).
  • Post-extubation support: high-risk (HF, COPD, age > 65, prolonged intubation) → HFNC + intermittent NIV (Thille 2019 reduced reintubation & mortality); average risk → HFNC alone is non-inferior.
  • Cuff pressure target 20–30 cmH₂O (checked q-shift, after manipulation); < 20 → aspiration + leak; > 30 → tracheal ischemia (capillary pressure ~ 25–30).
  • Tracheostomy indications: anticipated > 14 days ventilation, weaning failure, secretion burden, neuromuscular weakness, upper-airway obstruction; timing: no mortality difference early vs late — practical default re-evaluate day 10–14.

Bedside application

When the alarm goes off, the patient drops their sat, or the chest stops rising — the diagnostic algorithms in this section get you to a fix.

37Troubleshooting at the bedside

37.1 High-pressure alarm — DOPES / DOTTS

The mnemonic DOPES for sudden deterioration on the vent: Displaced ETT · Obstruction · Pneumothorax (PTX) · Equipment failure · Stacked breaths (auto-PEEP). The fix mnemonic is DOTTS: Disconnect from vent (bag with PEEP valve and FiO₂ 1.0) · Oxygenate · Tube position (waveform capnography + auscultation) · Tweak the vent (or check it) · Sonography (point-of-care ultrasound [PoCUS] for PTX, ETT depth, pleural sliding).

Concrete approach when PIP alarms:

  1. Look at PIP and Pplat. If PIP↑ but Pplat normal → resistance: suction, check ETT for kink/biting, consider bronchospasm, ETT cuff over-inflation.
  2. If PIP↑ and Pplat↑ → compliance: PTX (auscultate, PoCUS, chest X-ray [CXR]), atelectasis (mucus plug — bronchoscopy), pulmonary edema (CXR, B-type natriuretic peptide [BNP]), abdominal hypertension (bladder pressure).
  3. Address auto-PEEP if flow trace shows it (§16).

37.2 Low-pressure / low-VT alarm

  • Circuit disconnect — re-connect; check humidifier, water trap, ETT cuff.
  • Cuff leak — measure pilot pressure; re-inflate or replace tube.
  • Bronchopleural fistula (BPF) — refractory leak despite cuff; chest tube and consider high-frequency oscillatory ventilation (HFOV) / ECMO at center.

37.3 Acute hypoxemia on the vent

Move quickly. Bedside differential:

  • Tube — displaced, plugged, mainstem (auscultate, capnography, depth).
  • PTX — sliding lost on PoCUS, hyperresonance, hypotension.
  • Atelectasis — recent left lower-lobe collapse from a mucus plug; bronchoscopy.
  • Pulmonary edema — CXR, BNP, exam.
  • PE — high index of suspicion, especially if PaCO₂↓ and EtCO₂↓ with stable minute ventilation.
  • Right-to-left shunt opening — patent foramen ovale (PFO) from acute right-ventricle (RV) strain in PE / ARDS.
  • Asynchrony / fighting the vent — sedate.

37.4 Unexpected hypercapnia

  • ↑ CO₂ production: fever, sepsis, shivering, overfeeding (high respiratory quotient [RQ]), seizures.
  • ↑ dead space: PE, low cardiac-output state, high PEEP overdistension.
  • ↓ alveolar ventilation: auto-PEEP, leak, sedation, neuromuscular weakness, mode change.
  • Acidosis driving compensatory CO₂ retention is rare in mechanical ventilation; check pH and bicarbonate.
Key takeaways
  • High-pressure alarm (DOPES/DOTTS): Displaced ETT, Obstruction, Pneumothorax, Equipment, Stacked breaths (auto-PEEP) — Disconnect, Oxygenate, Tube position, Tweak vent, Sonography.
  • Diagnostic: if PIP↑ but Pplat normal → resistance (suction, kink, bronchospasm); if both ↑ → compliance (PTX, atelectasis, edema, abdominal hypertension).
  • Low-pressure/VT alarm: circuit disconnect, cuff leak (check pilot pressure), bronchopleural fistula (refractory leak).
  • Acute hypoxemia: tube (displaced, plugged, mainstem), PTX (no sliding on POCUS), atelectasis (mucus plug), edema, PE, PFO opening, asynchrony.

38Cases — putting it together

Eight teaching cases that walk the full reasoning chain — assessment, mechanism, vent strategy, anticipated trouble — for the archetype patients an ICU sees.

Case 1 — Severe ARDS, day 2

62F with influenza pneumonia, intubated 36 h ago. On VC-A/C: VT 380 mL (=6 mL/kg PBW), RR 26, PEEP 12, FiO₂ 0.90, set inspiratory flow 60 L/min, I:E 1:2.
ABG: pH 7.28, PaCO₂ 52, PaO₂ 70 → P/F 78. Pplat 32, total PEEP 13 (auto-PEEP 1), ΔP 20. Cstat = 380 / 20 = 19 mL/cmH₂O. Lactate 1.4. Norepi 0.06 µg/kg/min.

Reasoning: Severe diffuse ARDS (likely recruitable); driving pressure 20 is too high; Pplat at limit. Pure shunt physiology (P/F 78, FiO₂ 0.9 → still hypoxemic). Need to drop ΔP and recruit.

Plan (in order):

  1. Drop VT to 5 mL/kg PBW (≈ 320 mL); recheck Pplat at 5 min. Expected ΔP ~17.
  2. Increase PEEP per high-PEEP table: at FiO₂ 0.9, PEEP 18; check ΔP and SpO₂. If ΔP rises (overdistension), back off PEEP.
  3. Increase RR to 30 to compensate for VE loss (target pH ≥ 7.20).
  4. Sedate to RASS −3 to −4 (propofol + fentanyl). If persistent dyssynchrony, NMB cisatracurium 15 mg bolus + 37.5 mg/h × 24 h.
  5. Prone positioning ≥ 16 h/day (criteria met: P/F < 150 on FiO₂ ≥ 0.6 and PEEP ≥ 5).
  6. If P/F < 80 sustained after optimization + 2 prone sessions: ECMO referral.
  7. Conservative fluids (no resuscitation since shock controlled), Hb > 7, HOB 30°, head-of-bed care, DVT/stress-ulcer prophylaxis.

What to anticipate: prone-related desaturation in first 5 min (recovers); PEEP-induced hypotension (have norepi up-titration ready); P/F response in 1–4 h; failure to respond after 2 sessions = consider ECMO transfer.

Case 2 — Status asthmaticus, post-intubation crash

34M intubated 30 min ago for status asthmaticus. On VC-A/C: VT 500, RR 18, PEEP 5, FiO₂ 0.5, peak flow 60 L/min.
Now: BP 70/40, HR 130, SpO₂ 88. PIP 55, Pplat 22.

Reasoning: PIP–Pplat = 33 = resistance problem. Auto-PEEP/dynamic hyperinflation crashing venous return → hypotension. (Differential: tension PTX, but disconnect test will tell.)

Plan:

  1. Disconnect circuit, let chest empty 10 s. BP improves → confirms auto-PEEP. (If no improvement: needle decompress for PTX.)
  2. Reconnect with: RR 8–10, VT 6 mL/kg PBW (~ 420 mL), peak flow 80 L/min, I:E 1:4, square waveform, PEEP 0 initially (set to ~75% of measured auto-PEEP later if triggering).
  3. Volume bolus 500 mL crystalloid; norepi as needed.
  4. Continuous albuterol nebulizer 10–20 mg/h, ipratropium q4h.
  5. Methylprednisolone 80 mg IV; IV Mg 2 g over 20 min.
  6. Sedation to deep RASS −4 with ketamine + propofol; NMB if dyssynchrony.
  7. Permit hypercapnia: PaCO₂ 60–80 OK with pH ≥ 7.15.
  8. Reassess PIP, Pplat, auto-PEEP q1h. If pH < 7.10 despite optimization: ECMO referral.

Case 3 — COPD exacerbation on NIV, decision point at 2 h

72F, GOLD-D (Global Initiative for Chronic Obstructive Lung Disease, severity group D) COPD, exacerbation. On BiPAP IPAP 14 / EPAP 6, FiO₂ 0.4 × 2 h.
Started: pH 7.22, PaCO₂ 80, PaO₂ 58, RR 32, HR 110, GCS 13.
At 2 h: pH 7.32, PaCO₂ 60, PaO₂ 62, RR 22, HR 95, GCS 14.

Reasoning: Classic NIV success — pH up, PaCO₂ down, RR down, mentation improving. HACOR at 1 h was likely ≤ 5.

Plan: Continue BiPAP; same settings × 4–6 more h, then reassess. Plan to wean as tolerated. Treat exacerbation: nebs, steroids (prednisone 40 mg PO × 5 d), antibiotics if purulence, optimize home regimen for discharge.

Counterfactual: If at 2 h pH was 7.18 / PaCO₂ 85 / RR 36 / GCS 11 — intubate. Delay risks aspiration arrest. The 1–2 h reassessment is the safety net.

Case 4 — Cardiogenic edema, intubated despite NIV

78M, ischemic cardiomyopathy, EF 25%, presents with acute pulmonary edema, hypoxemia. NIV trial × 30 min: still SpO₂ 84% on FiO₂ 1.0, RR 38. Intubated.
Initial vent: VC-A/C, VT 6 mL/kg PBW, RR 18, PEEP 5, FiO₂ 1.0. ABG: pH 7.30, PaCO₂ 36, PaO₂ 65 → P/F 65. BP 95/55.

Reasoning: Cardiogenic shunt (flooded alveoli). PEEP recruits and reduces LV preload + afterload. Diuresis is the actual treatment.

Plan:

  1. Increase PEEP to 10–12 (afterload reduction, recruit). Reassess P/F at 30 min.
  2. Wean FiO₂ as oxygenation improves; target SpO₂ 92–96%.
  3. IV furosemide bolus + drip; goal net negative 2–3 L over 24 h.
  4. Inotropic support if needed (dobutamine if BP tolerates); revascularization if ischemic.
  5. Once euvolemic and EF recovering: rapid PSV transition, SBT within 24–48 h.
The post-extubation flash edema riskThis patient has marginal LV function. Post-extubation, the swing from positive to negative intrathoracic pressure raises LV afterload abruptly. Plan post-extubation NIV (IPAP 10/EPAP 5) and aggressive diuresis pre-extubation to avoid weaning-induced pulmonary edema (§36.4).

Case 5 — Septic shock + ARDS

54M, urinary sepsis, intubated. P/F 140 on PEEP 10 / FiO₂ 0.6. Norepi 0.4 µg/kg/min, vasopressin 0.04 U/min, lactate 4.5, MAP 62. Pplat 28, ΔP 13.

Reasoning: Moderate ARDS in distributive shock. Vent strategy is fine; the issue is competing physiology — PEEP titration may worsen shock; conservative fluids may worsen perfusion.

Plan:

  1. Lung-protective vent: continue VT 5–6 mL/kg, PEEP 10, FiO₂ to SpO₂ 88–95%, ΔP < 15. Acceptable as is.
  2. Source control: confirm urology consulted, drainage adequate, antibiotics broad-spectrum.
  3. Resuscitation: 30 mL/kg crystalloid completed; further volume only if dynamic markers (PPV, SVV, passive leg raise) suggest fluid responsiveness. Otherwise, hold fluids.
  4. Vasopressors: norepi titrated for MAP 65; add vasopressin (already on); epinephrine if refractory; consider hydrocortisone 200 mg/d if shock persists ≥ 6 h on max norepi.
  5. Once shock resolved (off pressors × 24 h): conservative fluid strategy (diurese to even balance per FACTT).
  6. Reassess for prone if P/F drops < 150.

Case 6 — Massive PE with shock

61F, post-op day 3 from total knee, sudden hypotension and hypoxemia. CT: saddle PE. Echo: dilated RV, septal flattening, McConnell's sign (apical sparing of RV free-wall hypokinesis — classic for acute PE). BP 80/50 on norepi 0.3 µg/kg/min, SpO₂ 88% on a non-rebreather (NRB) mask.

Reasoning: RV-led obstructive shock. Intubation will likely worsen RV (induction drops SVR; positive pressure drops preload). Consider catheter-directed therapy or thrombolysis before intubation.

Plan (escalating):

  1. HFNC 60 L/min FiO₂ 1.0 to maintain SpO₂ > 92% (avoid hypoxia → ↑ PVR).
  2. Norepi up; goal MAP 65 — RV coronary perfusion depends on systemic BP.
  3. Inhaled nitric oxide 20 ppm via mask if available — selectively drops PVR.
  4. Definitive: alteplase 100 mg IV over 2 h (or "half-dose" 50 mg if bleeding-prone), or catheter-directed thrombectomy at experienced center.
  5. If intubation unavoidable: pre-load norepi, ketamine + rocuronium, hand-bag low rate / low PEEP, have iNO and ECMO ready.
  6. VA-ECMO if shock refractory.

Case 7 — Guillain-Barré progression

43M, GBS day 4, on the ward. FVC has dropped from 3.2 → 1.8 L over 24 h. NIF −25 cmH₂O. RR 24, says "I'm tired."

Reasoning: Neuromuscular failure imminent. The 20–30–40 trigger is met (FVC dropping past threshold; NIF less negative than −30). Don't wait for ABG.

Plan:

  1. Transfer to ICU; intubate semi-electively (RSI with rocuronium — not succinylcholine: hyperkalemia risk in NMD).
  2. Vent: VC-A/C, VT 8–10 mL/kg PBW (lungs are normal, prevent atelectasis), PEEP 5, FiO₂ 0.3, RR matched to drive.
  3. Plasma exchange or IVIG.
  4. Aggressive secretion clearance — chest physiotherapy, in-exsufflator, suctioning.
  5. Plan tracheostomy at day 7–14 if no improvement in respiratory mechanics.
  6. DVT prophylaxis (immobile), nutrition, pressure-injury prevention, watch for autonomic instability (BP swings, arrhythmias).

Case 8 — Failure to wean post-CABG (coronary artery bypass graft)

71M, post-coronary artery bypass graft (CABG) day 5, persistent vent dependence. SBT today on PSV 5/PEEP 5 × 30 min: RR 28, VT 320, RSBI 88, but agitated and diaphoretic, BP rose 95 → 140, HR 80 → 110.

Reasoning: Numerical RSBI passes, patient fails. Look for fixable causes (the four classics: cardiac, fluid, neuro, mechanical).

Plan:

  1. Stop SBT, return to rest settings.
  2. Cardiac: echo for new wall-motion abnormality, troponin trend, BNP. SBT in marginal LV is a stress test.
  3. Fluid: net positive 6 L since OR. Diurese to even by tomorrow.
  4. Neuro: delirium screen (Confusion Assessment Method for the ICU [CAM-ICU]); minimize benzodiazepines; mobilize.
  5. Mechanical: cuff leak test for upper-airway edema; bronchoscopy for occult mucus plug or atelectasis; assess for diaphragm dysfunction (POCUS).
  6. Optimize nutrition (overfeeding raises VCO₂ → demand); treat pain (chest tubes); minimize nocturnal disruption.
  7. Re-attempt SBT in 24 h with above corrected. Discuss tracheostomy if vent dependence likely > 7 more days.
The "stress test" framing of every SBTAn SBT in a patient with marginal cardiac reserve, positive fluid balance, or weak diaphragm is exactly that: a stress test. Failure is information, not defeat — fix what failed, then retry. The biggest mistake is repeating the same SBT every day expecting a different result.

38.9 Worked calculation cases — the math at the bedside

Three patients at three points on the severity spectrum. For each: vignette, the bedside calculations, the changes that follow.

Case A — Mild ARDS, post-pneumonia, average build

Vignette. 68F, 5'4" (64 in), 80 kg actual, post-pneumonia ARDS. VC-A/C, VT 480 mL, RR 18, PEEP 8, FiO₂ 0.50. ABG pH 7.32 / PaCO₂ 50 / PaO₂ 88 (P/F 176). Pplat 24, PIP 28.

1. PBW (women) = \(45.5 + 2.3 \times (64 - 60) = 54.7\) kg.

2. VT/PBW = \(480 / 54.7 = 8.8\) mL/kg — too high (target 6 mL/kg = ~330 mL).

3. Driving pressure ΔP = \(P_{plat} - \text{PEEP} = 24 - 8 = 16\) cmH₂O — above target ≤ 15.

4. Mechanical power = \(0.098 \times 18 \times 0.480 \times (28 - \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 16) = 0.098 \times 18 \times 0.48 \times 20 \approx 16.9\) J/min — borderline elevated (concerning > 17).

Plan. Drop VT to 330 mL (6 mL/kg PBW) — this alone cuts MP and ΔP. Recheck Pplat in 1 h; if still ≥ 25, raise PEEP by 2 to recruit. If pH falls below 7.20, accept hypercapnia or escalate to deeper sedation.

Case B — Severe ARDS in obesity

Vignette. 55M, 5'10" (70 in), 145 kg actual (BMI 46), severe COVID ARDS, sedated and paralyzed. VC-A/C, VT 480 mL, RR 22, PEEP 14, FiO₂ 0.80. ABG pH 7.28 / PaCO₂ 55 / PaO₂ 76 (P/F 95). Pplat 32, PIP 40. No esophageal manometer available.

1. PBW (men) = \(50 + 2.3 \times (70 - 60) = 73\) kg. Use PBW, not actual weight — lungs don't scale with adiposity.

2. VT/PBW = \(480 / 73 = 6.6\) mL/kg — appropriate.

3. ΔP = \(32 - 14 = 18\) cmH₂O — elevated. Pplat 32 also exceeds the standard target.

4. The obesity caveat. In a BMI 46 patient, much of the airway pressure is chest-wall pressure, not alveolar pressure. With esophageal manometry you'd target PL,end-insp < 25; an "acceptable lung pressure" can correspond to a Pplat of 30–35. Without the manometer, accept Pplat up to ~35 if oxygenation and hemodynamics allow.

Plan. Don't drop VT further — that's not the problem. Initiate prone positioning ≥ 16 h/day. Place an esophageal balloon if available. Continue NMB while prone. If P/F stays < 80 despite optimization, ECMO consult. If improving on prone after 4–6 h, hold settings.

Case C — COPD exacerbation with auto-PEEP

Vignette. 72M, 5'8" (68 in), 70 kg, severe COPD. Just intubated. VC-A/C, set RR 14 (actual 24 — over-triggering), VT 600 mL, set PEEP 5, peak flow 60 L/min. ABG pH 7.22 / PaCO₂ 68 / PaO₂ 78. Pplat 20, PIP 28. Lightly sedated, agitated.

1. PBW = \(50 + 2.3 \times (68 - 60) = 68.4\) kg. VT/PBW = \(600/68.4 = 8.8\) mL/kg — slightly high but acceptable in COPD.

2. End-expiratory hold: total PEEP rises from 5 to 13. Auto-PEEP = \(13 - 5 = 8\) cmH₂O. Significant air-trapping.

3. True driving pressure (use total PEEP, not set PEEP) = \(P_{plat} - \text{total PEEP} = 20 - 13 = 7\) cmH₂O. Lung mechanics are fine — the patient's lungs aren't injured; the problem is incomplete exhalation.

4. PIP 28 vs Pplat 20 = a 8 cmH₂O resistive pressure (high airflow obstruction).

Plan.

  • Lengthen Te. Drop set RR to 12 (more time per breath to exhale).
  • Shorten Ti. Raise peak flow from 60 → 80 L/min (gets the breath in faster, frees expiratory time).
  • Set extrinsic PEEP ≈ 6 (~75% of auto-PEEP). Counterintuitive: adding PEEP reduces trigger work because the patient now starts each breath from 6 cmH₂O instead of 13.
  • Bronchodilators on full schedule (continuous albuterol or stacked nebs + ipratropium); steroids (methylprednisolone 60–125 mg IV q6 h).
  • Sedation — fentanyl + propofol — to take the edge off the air-trapping sensation.
  • Recheck end-expiratory hold in 30 min and ABG in 1 h. Goal pH > 7.25, falling auto-PEEP, slowing RR.
Key lesson from Case CIn auto-PEEP physiology, the danger is not high Pplat (it's normal) but ineffective triggering, RV strain, and patient distress. Fix it by giving the lung more time to exhale (low RR, high peak flow), not by lowering VT.
Key takeaways
  • Severe ARDS case: ↓VT to 5 mL/kg (ΔP too high), ↑PEEP per high-PEEP table (recruit), ↑RR to maintain VE, deep sedation ± NMB, prone ≥ 16 h/day, ECMO if P/F < 80 sustained.
  • Status asthmaticus crash: disconnect to decompress (confirms auto-PEEP), then revent: RR 8–10, VT 6 mL/kg, peak flow 80, I:E 1:4, PEEP 0 (later match ~75% auto-PEEP), permit PaCO₂ 60–80.
  • COPD exacerbation on NIV: if improving at 1–2 h (pH up, PaCO₂ down, RR down, GCS better) → continue; if worsening (pH ≤ 7.18, RR > 35) → intubate (aspiration arrest risk).
  • Cardiogenic edema: PEEP 8–12 for afterload reduction + recruitment, aggressive diuresis, inotropes if needed, rapid PSV transition; post-extubation NIV prophylaxis to prevent flash edema.

39Ventilator-associated events & the ABCDEF liberation bundle

The longer the ventilator stays, the more the patient is at risk for nosocomial pneumonia, ICU-acquired weakness, delirium, and post-ICU syndrome. Two frameworks have moved the needle.

39.1 Ventilator-associated events (VAEs) — the CDC surveillance ladder

TierDefinition
VAC (ventilator-associated condition)≥ 2 calendar days of worsening oxygenation after ≥ 2 days of stable / improving vent settings (FiO₂ ↑ ≥ 0.20 or PEEP ↑ ≥ 3 cmH₂O)
IVAC (infection-related VAC)VAC + temp < 36 or > 38 °C (or WBC < 4 or > 12 K/µL) + new antibiotic continued ≥ 4 days
PVAP (possible ventilator-associated pneumonia)IVAC + purulent secretions or positive respiratory culture meeting threshold

VAP (clinical) requires new infiltrate + 2 of: fever / leukocytosis / purulent secretions, with ≥ 48 h of intubation. Treat empirically per local antibiogram (typically anti-pseudomonal β-lactam ± MRSA coverage); de-escalate at 48–72 h.

39.2 VAP prevention bundle

  • Head of bed 30–45° unless contraindicated.
  • Daily oral care with chlorhexidine (controversial — many centers de-emphasize after 2017 meta-analyses; use cleaning rather than chlorhexidine).
  • Subglottic suction ETTs (continuous or intermittent) reduce VAP ~50%.
  • Cuff pressure 20–30 cmH₂O.
  • Daily SAT/SBT to shorten vent duration.
  • Stress-ulcer and DVT prophylaxis.
  • Avoid unnecessary circuit changes; change only if visibly soiled.

39.3 The ABCDEF bundle (ICU Liberation)

The Society of Critical Care Medicine's evidence-based bundle reduces delirium, mortality, and ICU/vent days when implemented coherently:22

39.4 ICU-acquired weakness (ICU-AW)

Up to 50% of patients ventilated > 7 days develop generalized weakness from disuse atrophy, critical illness myopathy, polyneuropathy, and prolonged NMB. Diaphragm-specific atrophy begins within 18 hours of controlled ventilation and is independently associated with prolonged liberation. Mitigation: minimize sedation, avoid unnecessary NMB, mobilize early, and use modes that preserve diaphragmatic activity (PSV, NAVA) where appropriate.

Over-sedation kills weeksThe single biggest reversible cause of prolonged ventilation is unnecessary deep sedation. Each day of light, targeted sedation (RASS 0 to −1) versus deep sedation roughly halves the duration of ventilation. Default to "less," not "more."
Key takeaways
  • VAE surveillance ladder: VAC → IVAC → PVAP. Each tier requires the previous + additional criteria.
  • Most reversible cause of prolonged ventilation: unnecessary deep sedation. Target RASS 0 to −1.
  • The ABCDEF bundle (Pain, Both SAT+SBT, Choice of meds, Delirium, Early mobility, Family) reduces mortality, vent days, and delirium when implemented coherently.
  • Diaphragm atrophy begins within 18 h of controlled ventilation. Preserve diaphragmatic activity when safe (PSV, NAVA).
  • Avoid benzodiazepine drips routinely — propofol or dexmedetomidine first-line.

How to read a portable ICU film in 30 seconds — the systematic look:
1. Lines & tubes: ETT 3–5 cm above carina, NGT through diaphragm, central line tip at SVC–RA junction, chest tube along the chest wall.
2. Heart silhouette: size and shape (cardiomegaly > 50% of thorax on good PA).
3. Mediastinum: shifted? widened?
4. Lungs: symmetric aeration; new opacities (alveolar vs interstitial); pneumothorax (deep sulcus on supine).
5. Pleura: blunted costophrenic angles, layering effusion.
6. Diaphragms & below: free air, NG position, gastric distension.
7. Bones & soft tissues: rib fractures, subcutaneous emphysema (a tip-off to occult pneumothorax).
Key takeaways
  • On a supine portable film, the deep sulcus sign is the pneumothorax of the ICU. Don't miss it.
  • ETT tip 3–5 cm above carina; almost all malpositions are right mainstem.
  • Lobar pneumonia has consolidation without volume loss; lobar atelectasis has volume loss with mediastinal shift toward the affected side.
  • Cardiogenic edema → bat-wing + Kerley B + cephalization + cardiomegaly. ARDS → bilateral diffuse infiltrates that spare the costophrenic angles and have a normal heart size.
  • CXR is insensitive for PE — use bedside echo for RV strain and CTPA for diagnosis.
  • Always read ICU films systematically: lines & tubes → heart → mediastinum → lungs → pleura → diaphragms → bones.

41.7 Circuit disconnect — emergency protocol

Disconnect is a true emergency: positive pressure and PEEP are lost; hypoxemia within seconds in lung-injured patients.

  1. Call for help.
  2. Pick up self-inflating bag; manually ventilate with 100% O₂ — confirms airway is patent.
  3. Visually trace circuit while bagging: Y-piece, HME, inspiratory limb, expiratory limb, exhalation valve, temperature probe port.
  4. Reconnect at the disconnection site.
  5. Restart vent; confirm PEEP, FiO₂, alarms re-armed.
  6. ABG and CXR if any concern for aspiration during the event.
